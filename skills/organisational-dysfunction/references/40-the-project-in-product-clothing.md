# The project in product clothing

*Dysfunction #40 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7467835377018535937-1LvX) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The organisation has "gone product": teams run sprints, retros, demos, the whole ceremony set.
- But the scope is locked and the deadline is real — the team is committed to deliver specific functionality by a fixed date.
- Halfway through, the team discovers the problem is more complex and different than anyone assumed — exactly the kind of learning product work is supposed to enable.
- There's nothing they can do with that learning: the steering committee, the scope, and the date don't bend.
- A familiar quiet cynicism settles in — the forms are agile, the logic is a Gantt chart.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

Projects and products are not two styles of the same activity; they rest on opposite theories of the problem itself. A project assumes the problem is known and the solution is specifiable, so the work is execution — a closed system: scope goes in, deliverable comes out, success is hitting the spec on time. A product assumes the problem is only partly known and the solution emerges through contact with the world — an open system that keeps transacting with users, market, and technology across its whole life. Wrapping the second in the rituals of the first doesn't blend them; it subordinates the open logic to the closed one.

Emery and Trist's causal-texture framework names the mismatch precisely. Fixed scope, a steering committee, and a delivery date are Type III instruments — appropriate where the environment is clustered and reasonably predictable. Product development lives in the turbulent Type IV field, where the ground moves under you. Govern Type IV work with Type III instruments and you strip out exactly the adaptive capacity that product thinking exists to create. The team can sense the environment shifting but is structurally forbidden from responding.

So the dysfunction isn't that the team is bad at product. It's that "product" has been reduced to vocabulary and ceremony while the load-bearing structure — who controls scope, when, against what constraints — is still pure project. Half a transformation, and arguably worse than none, because it discredits the very idea it pretends to embody.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — give product work an open-system governance, or stop calling it product.**
- Replace fixed scope-and-date commitments with outcome and problem ownership; let scope flex as understanding grows.
- Retire the steering committee as a scope-policing body; move the authority to adapt to the team in contact with the environment.
- Fund the work as a continuing capability, not a one-off delivery with an end date.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Make the locked constraints explicit up front so the team isn't blamed for the closed system it's been handed.
- Negotiate scope flex even within a fixed date — protect the team's ability to act on what it learns mid-stream.
- Reframe the cynicism: the team isn't failing at agile; it's being asked to run an open system on closed-system rails.
