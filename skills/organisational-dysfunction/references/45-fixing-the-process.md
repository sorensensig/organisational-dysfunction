# Fixing the process

*Dysfunction #45 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7470357877627731970-HkKz) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- You run Scrum and Lean by the book — standups, retros, ceremonies all on time — and the same problems keep coming back wearing new labels.
- Early adoption gave a real bump, then everything plateaued and old patterns crept back in.
- Standups have quietly degraded into status reporting; retros surface the same issues quarter after quarter.
- The honest summary in the room is: "the process is being followed, and the problems are still there."

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The Lean and Scrum traditions did something genuinely useful — they made flow visible and tightened feedback loops. But they stopped at process. They optimise *how work moves* without touching *who is allowed to decide what*. That ceiling is structural: you can perfect the workflow inside a DP1 organisation and still be inside a DP1 organisation, where coordination and control live one level above the people doing the work.

This is why the gains plateau. The constraints that actually bind the team — who sets the goal, who can change priorities, who owns the trade-offs — are invisible to a process improvement because they sit above the team's authority. A better ceremony cannot relocate decision rights. So the team keeps polishing the part it can touch while the binding constraint sits untouched, and the dysfunction regenerates faster than the ritual can suppress it.

The deeper trap is mistaking the symptom for the disease. "Our process needs improving" feels actionable and blameless, so organisations sink years into it. But a process layered over a DP1 structure is still DP1; you have decorated the constraint, not removed it.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — change the design principle, not the ceremony.**
- Move decision rights down: let the team that owns the work own the goals, priorities, and trade-offs for that work.
- Use Participative Design Workshops to deliberately shift from DP1 (control above the work) to DP2 (control with the work) — the people doing the work redesign their own structure.
- Look for the order-of-magnitude results that only structural change unlocks (e.g. lead times collapsing from days to hours), not the marginal gains process tuning yields.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Name the ceiling out loud: distinguish, in your retro, problems the team can actually fix from ones blocked by decisions held above it. Stop spending retro energy on the latter.
- Take the list of structurally-blocked items upward as evidence, not complaints — make the constraint visible to whoever does hold the authority.
- Reframe for the team: the plateau is not a failure of discipline or motivation. You've optimised everything inside your authority; the rest needs a different lever.
