# Psychological safety as a patch

*Dysfunction `#17` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7453819051090427904-MJjr) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The org runs psychological-safety workshops, adds it to onboarding, surveys for it, and coaches leaders on it.
- People still don't speak up, still don't take risks, still don't challenge decisions made above them.
- Survey scores wobble but behaviour doesn't change; the "safety" stays on the poster, not in the room.
- Leaders conclude their people need more training, more reassurance, more encouragement — and run the cycle again.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

Psychological safety is being treated as a feature you can install — a programme to roll out. It isn't. It's an emergent property of the structure people actually work in. You can't bolt it onto a structure that systematically produces its opposite.

In a DP1 hierarchy, your position is subordinate, and the rational response to sitting below someone who controls your work, evaluation, and standing is caution. Bion's basic assumptions — dependency, fight/flight, factionalism — are the *natural* human response to autocratic structures, not defects in the individuals exhibiting them. Holding your tongue in front of the person who decides your fate isn't a courage gap; it's an accurate reading of the power gradient. Training people to "feel safe" while the gradient remains is asking them to ignore something true about their situation.

DP2 produces psychological safety as a by-product, because there's no steep gradient to be unsafe across: people are peers, designing and owning their own work, negotiating as equals. The safety isn't trained in — it falls out of the structure. Which is exactly why no amount of coaching dissolves the fear while the hierarchy that generates it stays in place.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — flatten the power gradient that makes speaking up irrational.**
- Move toward peer-based, self-managing teams that own their own work, so safety emerges from the structure rather than from a programme.
- Stop trying to train people out of a response the structure keeps producing; change the conditions that produce it.
- Treat low psychological safety as a readout of the hierarchy, not as a deficiency in the people scoring it.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- As a leader, deliberately and visibly reduce your own use of positional power — but be honest that this softens, not removes, the gradient.
- Decouple speaking-up from evaluation where you can, so dissent isn't routed to the person who rates you.
- Reframe "they won't speak up" for peers and your own boss: it's a rational read of power, not a training problem to be patched.
