# The short-termism machine

*Dysfunction #58 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7477235686706962432-acP8) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The company professes long-term values and a five-year strategy, then decides for the next quarter every time.
- Investment in capability gets deferred; technical debt quietly accumulates.
- People get restructured rather than developed, because development pays off on a timescale nobody is measured on.
- Leaders move on, and the consequences of their short-term calls land on whoever inherits the seat.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

This is a structural design problem, not a cultural failing or a shortage of far-sighted leaders. In a DP1 hierarchy, individual executives rationally optimise for their own tenure and personal metrics, because their position, bonus, and reputation are all measured on timescales that don't reward patience. The organisation's long-term health and the individual's incentives align only by accident. When a leader leaves, the consequences become someone else's problem — so the discount rate on the future is, structurally, very high.

Chasing this with values statements or exhortations to "think long term" is hopeless, because you're asking individuals to override the very incentives the structure selects them on. The behaviour isn't a character flaw in the executives; it's exactly what the design rewards. Short-termism is not human nature — it's what DP1 selects for.

DP2 changes the selection pressure. When a self-managing group collectively owns purpose and outcomes, nobody in it is positioning for the next job, and the consequences of decisions stay with the people who made them. Dee Hock's VISA is the example: an organisation deliberately structured to outlast any individual within it, so its decisions weren't hostage to anyone's personal timeline.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — move ownership of purpose and outcomes to a collective that lives with its own consequences.**
- Shift from individual executive incentives to collective ownership of purpose, so the people deciding are the people who inherit the results.
- Design so that consequences stay with their authors rather than being passed to a successor.
- Build for continuity over individual tenure — structure the organisation to outlast any one person in it.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Make the timescale mismatch visible: show where decisions optimise a personal horizon at the system's long-term expense.
- Defend a small set of capability investments explicitly, framed as protecting the people who'll inherit the consequences.
- Reframe the behaviour: stop blaming "short-sighted leadership" and name the incentive structure that selects for it, so the conversation moves to design.
