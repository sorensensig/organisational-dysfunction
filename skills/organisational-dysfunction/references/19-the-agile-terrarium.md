# The agile terrarium

*Dysfunction #19 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7455915112764223488-WAlg) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The team's agile process runs fine on its own terms — ceremonies happen, the board moves — but it's sealed off from the organisation around it.
- A stable, curated backlog protects the team from the turbulence the rest of the business actually lives in.
- After decades of adoption, the promised outcomes — engaged, self-managing, high-performing teams — largely haven't materialised.
- The process works; the organisation around it doesn't, and the gap is invisible from inside the glass.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

An agile team in a terrarium is a *closed* system: insulated from its environment, fed a stable backlog, optimising internal flow. But an organisation is an *open* system — it survives only by engaging with a turbulent environment, learning from it, and adapting. Sealing a team away from that turbulence makes its metrics look healthy while disconnecting it from the only thing that makes adaptation possible.

The structural root is a coordination vacuum. Agile's founders correctly identified rigid supervision as the problem and removed it — but never defined what should replace it. Strip out hierarchical control (DP1) without installing genuine peer-based self-management (DP2) and you don't get autonomy; you get laissez-faire — neither bureaucratic nor truly self-managing. The research bears this out: a majority of respondents show predominantly high laissez-faire leadership. The supervision left, but coordination never arrived.

So agile isn't failing because the process is wrong. It's failing because a closed system can't adapt, and the industry has spent years trying to solve an open-systems problem with a delivery methodology. As one commenter noted, the hard part is that protection and abandonment look identical from inside the team — which is exactly why the terrarium is so comfortable and so quietly fatal.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — replace the missing coordination with real DP2, and open the team to its environment.**
- Install genuine peer-based self-management to fill the coordination vacuum, rather than leaving the team in laissez-faire limbo.
- Connect the team to its actual environment — let real turbulence and feedback reach it instead of a curated, stabilised backlog.
- Design the organisation *around* the team as an open system that learns and adapts, not just the team's internal process.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Deliberately puncture the glass: pull live signals from customers and the wider business directly to the team, past the curated backlog.
- Build real coordination links to neighbouring teams yourself, so "autonomy" doesn't quietly mean abandonment.
- Name the terrarium for what it is — comfortable insulation can read as success right up until adaptation is needed and isn't there.
