# The output nobody owned

*Dysfunction `#60` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7477964427401814016-uuih) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- A workshop generates hundreds of post-its, which the facilitators cluster and synthesise into a clean, polished summary.
- Participants can't actually trace what happened to their own contributions — whether they survived, got merged into something else, were reworded, or quietly vanished.
- The output *looks* authoritative, but the trust in it is thinner than it looks: people nod along without really owning the result.
- Weeks later, nobody feels bound by the "outcome" because nobody is sure it reflects what the room actually said.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The problem isn't sloppy facilitation — it's that the medium itself breaks the conditions for genuine shared agreement. Post-its can be removed, merged, reworded, or lost **without anyone noticing**. That quietly violates two of Solomon Asch's four conditions for effective communication: **openness** and a **mutually shared field**. Once the record can be curated out of sight, the field is no longer shared or open — it's a synthesis handed down, however well-intentioned.

This is a DP1 move hiding inside a participative ritual: the facilitators (a layer above the group) become the integrators of everyone's fragments, and the group has to trust a curation it can't see. The appearance of participation is preserved while the actual authority over the shared picture sits with whoever holds the pen.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — make the shared record impossible to alter unseen, and keep authorship with the authors.**
- Use the Search Conference tradition: write everything on large sheets of butcher's paper fixed to the walls, kept visible for the whole session. Merrelyn Emery treated butcher's paper as the ultimate guarantee of openness precisely because it's virtually impossible to change without the room noticing.
- Have each group consolidate its *own* work and present it, with the authors in the room to verify it — rather than facilitators synthesising on the group's behalf.

**If you can't change the whole format yet:**
- At minimum, keep the raw record visible and unedited alongside any synthesis, so people can check their contribution survived.
- When you must cluster or merge, do it live and out loud, with the authors present, not offline afterwards.
