# People resist change

*Dysfunction `#35` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7465295955588886531-dQg0) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- A transformation stalls, and leadership reaches for the stock diagnosis: people resist change.
- The story is cultural — staff are comfortable, risk-averse, slow to let go of the old ways.
- The response is a comms campaign and training to make everyone more "comfortable with uncertainty."
- More is communicated *at* people; the resistance hardens.
- Engagement scores drop, and that's read as further proof the workforce is the problem.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The premise is just false. When people are asked, the overwhelming majority *want* change — in one software-industry survey, over 80% of people in organisations not currently changing wished they were. What people reject is not change. It's change *imposed on them*, which is an entirely different thing. The resistance is real; it's aimed at the imposition, not the destination.

So the dysfunction is recursive: a DP1 organisation hands change down from above, hits the predictable pushback, and reaches for *more* DP1 — bigger campaigns, more stakeholder management, more being-told. It is treating a symptom of top-down control with additional top-down control, and is genuinely puzzled when the dose makes the patient worse. When people help *design* the change, resistance largely evaporates, because there is nothing left to resist — the change is theirs.

That single insight is the whole game. "Resistance" is the signal that the people who will live with the change had no hand in shaping it. Chase the resistance with persuasion and you confirm exactly the powerlessness that created it.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — let the people who will live with the change design it; ownership dissolves resistance because there's nothing left to resist.**
- Run genuine participative methods — Search Conferences, Participative Design Workshops — where those affected do the designing, not just receive the rollout.
- Replace "stakeholder management" (managing people's reactions to a decision already made) with real authorship of the decision.
- Treat involvement as the mechanism of change, not a communications garnish on top of it.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Push to widen participation on whatever slice you control — let your own team design its part of the change for real.
- When you hear "they're resisting," reframe it: people are responding rationally to having change done *to* them.
- Stop spending budget on persuasion and spend that effort on giving people an actual say; one converts, the other inflames.
