# The powerless retrospective

*Dysfunction #3 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-agile-share-7447399664334794752-HLCX) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The team runs a solid retro, generates good improvement ideas — and then notices most of them are outside its control.
- Actions get written down as "escalate to management" or "raise with the department," then disappear.
- The same issues reappear retro after retro because the levers that would fix them sit elsewhere.
- People stop bringing the big problems and shrink the conversation to the few things they *can* touch (coffee, meeting times, ticket hygiene).
- Attendance and energy decay; the ritual starts to feel like going through the motions.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

A retrospective assumes a DP2 world: a team that can inspect its own work and then *change* it. Run that ritual inside a DP1 structure, where the decisions that matter are held above the team, and you get a machine for surfacing problems the team is powerless to solve. The format promises agency the org design never granted.

This is the gap between ceremonial voice and real authority. The team is told it is self-managing, but as Hjorteland puts it, it cannot change what actually matters to it. So the retro becomes theatre — a place to *talk* about improvement that has been carefully walled off from the power to enact it. Chasing better facilitation, sharper formats, or more disciplined follow-up misses the point: the constraint isn't the meeting, it's where authority lives.

Worse, a powerless retro actively teaches learned helplessness. Each cycle where good ideas die upstream trains the team that raising real problems is pointless, which is precisely the disengagement leaders then blame on the team.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — give the team authority over the things its retro keeps surfacing.**
- Move decision rights down so that the improvements the team identifies are mostly the team's to implement (DP2).
- For issues genuinely above the team, create a real, accountable channel with a named owner and a response — not an escalation void.
- Track the *fate* of retro actions: if most route to "someone else," that's the diagnosis. Redesign the boundary, not the meeting.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Split the board honestly into "ours to fix" and "needs power we don't have" — act fully on the first, and stop pretending the second is an action item.
- Aggregate the cross-boundary items and take them up as a pattern, with cost attached, rather than as scattered complaints; make the structural blocker visible and expensive to ignore.
- Tell the team plainly: the dead actions aren't your failure, they're evidence the authority is misplaced. That reframing protects morale and keeps people honest about where the fix has to come from.
