# The company's strategy is unclear

*Dysfunction #10 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7450079065987887104-jLDM) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Leadership is frustrated that people are "not aligned," while the people themselves say they have no clear basis for day-to-day decisions.
- The strategy has been broadcast everywhere — town halls, intranet pages, leadership updates, posters in the kitchen — and still nobody can act on it.
- The slide deck reads as lofty ambition ("be the leading X", "delight our customers") that nobody can translate into what to actually do on Tuesday.
- Each new "communication push" lands with a thud; the assumption is that people didn't hear it, so the answer is to say it again, louder.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The reflex is to treat this as a communication problem — message not delivered, so re-deliver. That misreads it. A strategy fails not because it was under-broadcast but because it never made *operational sense* to the people expected to enact it. It has to give their work a relevant, relatable purpose; a goal that lives only at the top of the org provides no guidance at the point of work.

This is the DP1 cascade at work. In a redundancy-of-parts structure, strategy is set one level up and pushed down as an announcement to be received and obeyed. But meaning can't be cascaded the way an instruction can. People closest to the work hold the context that would make the strategy concrete, and that context is exactly what a top-down broadcast strips out. The louder-communication remedy is Goodhart-flavoured: it optimises the visible proxy (reach, message frequency) while the real variable — whether the strategy connects to lived work — goes untouched.

So the disengagement isn't apathy or "not getting it." It's the rational response to being handed a goal with no line back to your own work. Repeating the message can't fix a structural disconnect between where strategy is made and where it has to mean something.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — people make sense of a strategy they helped shape, not one they were told.**
- Bring the people who do the work into strategy formation, so direction and operational meaning are produced together rather than translated after the fact.
- Where the org is too large for everyone to be in the room, seed trusted people throughout it who genuinely co-own the direction — not messengers, but participants others relate to.
- Push goal-setting down to where the work happens, so each part can derive a concrete, relevant purpose from the shared direction.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- As a middle manager, act as a translator: turn the corporate abstraction into something actionable and relevant for your people, and carry their reality back up.
- Run a session with your team to co-write what the strategy means for *your* work specifically — local meaning beats another all-hands.
- Reframe the "they don't get it" narrative: people aren't failing to listen; they've been given nothing concrete to act on.
