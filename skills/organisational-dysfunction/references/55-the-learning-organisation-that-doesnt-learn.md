# The learning organisation that doesn't learn

*Dysfunction `#55` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7475431307372728320-DC11) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Heavy investment in learning infrastructure — lunch-and-learns, communities of practice, an LMS, conference budgets — yet how the work actually gets done barely changes.
- Retrospectives surface the same issues quarter after quarter, and the same actions die quietly.
- People learn plenty as individuals; none of it propagates into the system.
- "We're a learning organisation" is a slogan, while the working process is frozen.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

Genuine organisational learning requires three things: teams setting their own goals, getting direct feedback on the work, and changing the work in response without waiting for approval. A DP1 structure systematically blocks all three. Goals arrive from above. Feedback has to travel up the hierarchy and back down before anyone can act. Changing how the work is done needs management sign-off. So learning gets divorced from doing — knowledge accumulates in people's heads and in slide decks, but never closes the loop into changed practice.

That is why the LMS and the community of practice don't fix it. They aren't the problem; they're the symptom of an organisation trying to *buy* learning that its structure prevents. You can pour content in at the top, but the structure has no mechanism to convert insight into altered work at the point where the work happens. The retrospective naming the same issue every quarter is the clearest tell: the team can see the problem perfectly and is structurally unable to act on it.

In DP2 this inverts. When teams own their goals, hold their own feedback loops, and can adjust their work directly, learning stops being a programme you attend and becomes what the structure produces continuously. Learning is an output of the design, not an initiative bolted onto it.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — give the loop somewhere to close by putting goals, feedback, and the authority to change the work with the team.**
- Let teams set and own their goals rather than receiving them; learning has no purchase when the target is external and fixed.
- Bring feedback directly to the people doing the work, not up-and-back through the hierarchy.
- Grant teams the authority to change their own working process without seeking approval — that is the step that turns insight into changed practice.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Stop measuring learning by attendance and content consumed; measure whether anything in the work actually changed.
- Give your retro a small ringfenced set of actions the team is genuinely allowed to implement, so it stops re-diagnosing the unfixable.
- Reframe the failure: it isn't that people won't learn or apply it — the structure gives the learning nowhere to land.
