# Career paths

*Dysfunction #52 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7473608033110167552-O-dm) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- A talented developer faces a fork: climb the management ladder away from the work they're good at, or stay technical and hit a ceiling.
- People take leadership roles reluctantly — not because they want to manage anyone, but because it's the only way "up."
- The deepest practitioners get pulled away from the craft just as they become most valuable at it.
- "Advancement" is defined entirely as ascent through the hierarchy; doing the actual work better isn't a recognised path.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The bureaucratic career ladder is one of the main mechanisms that keeps DP1 in place psychologically. A DP1 structure needs people to accept that worth, status, and reward are distributed by *level* — and the career ladder is how that belief gets installed and maintained. It takes a coordination need (someone has to integrate the fragments) and converts it into an individual incentive (climb, or stagnate), so that ambition itself pulls people up into control layers and away from the work.

That conversion is the quiet damage. It systematically removes your best practitioners from practice, because the only sanctioned way to grow is to stop doing the thing you're growing at. And it locks the design principle in: as long as the sole route to more money and status runs through management, everyone has a personal stake in the hierarchy continuing to exist. The ladder isn't just a side effect of DP1 — it's part of what holds DP1 up.

A DP2 organisation doesn't have that hierarchy to climb, so its notion of growth has to be different in kind, not just have an extra "technical track" stapled to the same ladder.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — define advancement as depth of skill and breadth of contribution, not ascent through a hierarchy DP2 doesn't have.**
- Build recognition and reward around mastering the craft and widening the scope of what you contribute, rather than around moving up levels.
- Decouple money and status from managerial position, so people aren't forced into control roles to progress.
- Treat coordination as a function the group shares, not a rung that pulls your best people out of the work.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Be honest in the transition: people invested in the old ladder stand to lose status, and if you don't address that expectation directly the change will stall. Name it.
- Create local recognition for depth and breadth even before HR catches up — make craft growth visible and valued on your team.
- Reframe for the reluctant manager-to-be: wanting to keep doing the work isn't a lack of ambition; the structure simply offers no other way to grow, and that's the thing that's broken.
