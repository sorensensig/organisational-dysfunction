# Permanent urgency

*Dysfunction #56 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-ugcPost-7475784893109751808-KqZ4) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Everything is a top priority, all the time; the word "urgent" has stopped meaning anything.
- Constant context-switching, lots of work started and barely finished, nothing ever properly done.
- No slack for reflection, refactoring, or learning — the moment one fire is out, the next is already lit.
- A tiredness that rest doesn't fix, because the pace never actually relents.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

A DP1 structure has no built-in mechanism for deciding what *not* to do. Prioritisation happens above the team, and the people above keep labelling everything critical — so the team has no structural ability to push back. It can only absorb. There is no off switch because the team owns delivery but not goals: it is told what matters and has no standing to say "not this, not now." Permanent urgency is the predictable steady state of that arrangement, not a temporary crunch.

This violates a basic psychological requirement of productive work: people need adequate variety and continual learning, and both depend on having some uncommitted time. A system run permanently at full capacity has no room for the very activities that keep it capable. So capability quietly degrades — technical debt builds, skills don't refresh, and the team gets slower while being pushed to go faster.

The trap is that the urgency feels like a fact about the world ("we're just very busy") when it is a fact about the structure. Nobody owns the decision to stop, so nobody makes it. Treating it as a personal resilience or time-management issue — telling people to prioritise better or protect their energy — misreads a structural absence as an individual failing.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — create an owner and a mechanism for deciding what not to do.**
- Give teams ownership of goals, not just delivery, so they have the standing to push back and de-scope.
- Build an explicit "off switch": a structural way to take work off the list, not just add to it.
- Protect uncommitted capacity by design — treat slack for learning and improvement as part of the system, not a luxury to be raided.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Make the cost of "everything is urgent" visible: surface the WIP, the switching, and the unfinished work so the trade-off is undeniable.
- Force prioritisation upward by asking which item should be dropped to take a new one — make the no someone else's explicit decision.
- Reframe the exhaustion as a structural signal, not a stamina problem, so people stop blaming themselves for a pace the design guarantees.
