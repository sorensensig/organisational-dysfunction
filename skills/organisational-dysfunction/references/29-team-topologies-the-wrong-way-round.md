# Team Topologies, the wrong way round

*Dysfunction #29 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7462030466523590657-VIuu) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Teams have been renamed into stream-aligned, platform, enabling and complicated-subsystem types — and six months on, nothing has moved.
- "Stream-aligned" teams still wait for an architect to approve their design and a manager to tell them what to build next.
- The platform team behaves like a ticket-driven service desk, not a product team with users.
- Enabling-team workshops are scheduled, and nobody shows up.
- Cognitive load is exactly where it was; delivery is no faster. The map changed; the territory didn't.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

Team Topologies is a DP2 idea: it presupposes teams that own a whole stream of value, set their own goals, and coordinate peer-to-peer, with cognitive load as the thing you design *around* rather than *assign*. Its team types only make sense if authority already sits with the people doing the work.

What organisations actually do is impose the topology from above while leaving the design principle untouched. Drawing the new boxes is itself a DP1 act — coordination and decision rights still live one level up. So a self-managing framework gets bolted onto a command-and-control spine. A stream-aligned team with no real autonomy is just a delivery unit with a fashionable label; an enabling team with no peer relationship to enable is a staff function nobody asked for.

Then Goodhart finishes the job. Once "adopt Team Topologies" becomes the target the organisation is measured against, the topology stops describing how work flows and starts describing how the reorg was reported upward. You get the diagram without the dynamics it was supposed to name.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — move decision rights into the teams *before* you draw their boundaries, not after.**
- Shift goal-setting, design approval and prioritisation down to the stream-aligned teams; if an architect or manager still gates the work, the topology is cosmetic.
- Make the platform a product with internal users and its own roadmap, not a queue.
- Let team boundaries follow from where whole pieces of work actually live, so cognitive load is genuinely contained rather than relabelled.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Don't sell the rename as transformation — name out loud that the topology is a target the structure can't yet honour.
- Use the vocabulary to surface specific blockers ("we're called stream-aligned but we can't decide X") — concrete friction is more persuasive than abstract autonomy.
- Stop blaming teams for "not embracing the model"; their behaviour is the rational read of who still holds the authority.
