# The customer we never met

*Dysfunction `#33` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7463491390904242176-nFFw) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Engineers make hundreds of product decisions a week and have never once spoken to a customer.
- All contact with the user is mediated: the product owner translates needs into requirements, UX translates behaviour into insights, and the team receives only the output.
- Understanding of the customer arrives as personas, Jira tickets and dashboards — abstractions about people, not the people.
- Debates are settled by appeal to the spec, because nobody in the room has the lived context to settle them otherwise.
- The team is busy and confident, and quietly building for a customer it has never met.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

An open system stays alive through unmediated contact with its environment. For a product team, the customer *is* the environment. Insert a chain of translators between the team and that environment and you've built a closed system — one that runs on internal representations instead of live signal. It will feel orderly right up until it's wrong, because it has no direct way to find out it's wrong.

Each translation in the chain is lossy, and not randomly so. What gets stripped is exactly the high-value part: the judgement, the context, the friction, the moment a real person says something that reframes the whole problem. By the time a need has been rendered into a requirement and the requirement into a ticket, the thing that would have changed your mind is gone. The dashboard preserves the measurable and discards the surprising.

This is DP1 logic applied to knowing your user: the whole task has been fragmented and the "understanding the customer" piece handed to a specialist role, to be reintegrated upward. But understanding who the work is for isn't a separable sub-task you can delegate — it's constitutive of doing the work well. A DP2 team owns the *whole* task, and the whole task includes meeting the people it serves.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — give the team direct, unmediated contact with its customers; that's a condition for staying alive, not a nice-to-have.**
- Make "understanding who this is for" part of the team's owned task, not a role that briefs them.
- Build standing, direct customer access — the team talks to real users routinely, not via a relay.
- Keep PO and UX as amplifiers and synthesisers of contact the team already has, not as the team's only window onto reality.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Get engineers into customer calls, support queues and research sessions as observers — even read-only contact beats secondhand summaries.
- Treat personas and tickets as lossy proxies and say so; chase the surprising signal the abstractions dropped.
- Don't accept "that's the PO's job" as the end of it — the team that touches the customer decides differently from the team that doesn't.
