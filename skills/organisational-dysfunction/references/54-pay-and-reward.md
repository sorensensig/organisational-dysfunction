# Pay and reward

*Dysfunction #54 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-ugcPost-7471869018451382272-dW9J) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- You build genuine team accountability over a year, then the first individual bonus cycle quietly corrodes it.
- People start making sure their name is on the visible, rateable work; quiet glue work gets dropped.
- The annual rating conversation turns colleagues into competitors right when the team needed them collaborating.
- Values posters say "one team", but everyone privately optimises for their personal target.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The teams are designed around shared accountability, but the reward system still runs on DP1 assumptions: individual performance ratings, personal targets, bonuses tied to what one named person delivered. When the structure says "collective" and the pay system says "individual", the pay system wins. Reward is a hard, recurring, money-backed signal; a team charter is words. People aren't being greedy — they're reading the strongest signal in the system and responding rationally.

This is a textbook collision between a DP2 intent and a DP1 mechanism. Individual ranking, which feels normal and even fair under hierarchy, is in a genuinely self-managing group not just unnecessary but structurally corrosive: it manufactures the very internal competition the team design was meant to dissolve. Trying to fix it with culture work or "be more collaborative" coaching is chasing the symptom while leaving the cause — Goodhart in reverse, where you preach the goal you simultaneously pay against.

The deeper point: you cannot have collective behaviour and individual reward at the same time. One of them has to give, and if you don't redesign reward, reward will redesign your teams back into individuals.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — pay for skill, not for position or personal output.**
- Compensate people for the range of functions they can perform for the group, so reward incentivises learning and broad contribution rather than name-claiming.
- Remove individual ranking from self-managing teams; move recognition and any variable reward to the team and the whole work it owns.
- Align the reward system with the team structure deliberately — treat it as part of the redesign, not an HR afterthought.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Make the conflict explicit so eroding collaboration is read as a reward-design problem, not a sign the team "isn't gelling".
- Where you have latitude, weight team outcomes and peer contribution in whatever rating process you're stuck with.
- Protect the glue work openly — name and credit it in the room — so the unrewarded essential work doesn't silently evaporate.
