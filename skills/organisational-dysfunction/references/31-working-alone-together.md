# Working alone together

*Dysfunction #31 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7462755285229752320-kjV5) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Given a free choice, a large share of people would rather work from home — and dig in when called back to the office.
- The argument is fought over productivity and work-life balance, as if those were the real variables.
- In the office, people sit metres apart and still work essentially alone: proximity without connection.
- "Collaboration" means meetings and handoffs, not actually doing the work together.
- Mandating attendance doesn't create the togetherness leaders imagined it would; it just relocates solitary work.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The preference to work alone is not a personality fact about the workforce; it's an accurate read of how the work is designed. Under DP1, work is individualised by construction: tasks are broken into the smallest assignable pieces, handed to single people, and reintegrated by a manager. If your job is a fragment that only the hierarchy stitches into a whole, then being in a room with colleagues adds noise, not value — so of course you'd rather do it in peace.

Hjorteland reads this against the psychological requirements for productive work, and DP1 structurally starves two of them: mutual support and respect, and continual learning through real feedback. You can't get either from people whose work doesn't actually meet yours. The office can't manufacture connection that the task design has already removed.

In DP2 the group is the unit of work — coordination, learning and support happen *inside* the team because the team owns a whole piece of the work together. There, people want to be near each other, because the group is where the work lives. So the return-to-office war is really a design problem wearing a real-estate costume: we're debating *where* to put people instead of *how* their work is divided.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — make the group the unit of work, so togetherness is what the task requires, not what the policy mandates.**
- Redesign around whole tasks owned by a team, so coordination, feedback and support are intrinsic to getting the work done.
- Build mutual support and real feedback loops into the work itself, not into an attendance rule.
- Judge office time by whether the work genuinely needs people together — not by occupancy targets.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Don't read the WFH preference as disengagement; it's a rational response to individualised work.
- Create genuine shared work — pairing, mob sessions, collective ownership of a slice — that makes co-presence worthwhile when it happens.
- Stop trying to win connection with a mandate; name that the work is currently designed to be done alone.
