# Involvement theatre

*Dysfunction `#28` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7460943304956846080--OsL) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- A genuinely collaborative session is run — EventStorming, a design sprint, a co-design workshop — with real energy and real participation.
- Then the actual design is reworked behind closed doors. The document that lands in the team's inbox looks nothing like what people thought they'd built together.
- The team realises the workshop was consultation dressed up as co-creation: their input was *heard*, but the authority to decide never left the room above them.
- Next time, participation is hollow. People show up, go through the motions, and save their real thinking for after the meeting — because experience taught them it won't count.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

Two structural mechanisms wreck the workshop, and neither is fixable with better facilitation. First, **authority dynamics**: the moment someone with authority is in the room — even a well-meaning, independent facilitator carries some of this — the group shifts into Bion's basic-assumption modes, dependency or fight/flight, instead of genuine collaborative work. The presence of authority changes the behaviour regardless of how skilled the facilitator is.

Second, the **ownership gap** (Emery & Emery): people develop the motivation, responsibility, and commitment to implement something only when they have designed *their own* work. A design handed down — however participatively it was "informed" — doesn't carry that ownership, because the group didn't actually decide it. Involvement theatre is one of the most common misapplications of participative methods in **DP1** organisations: design authority is retained above, and the *appearance* of participation is layered on top.

That layering is the whole problem. Genuine participation isn't a technique you add to a DP1 structure; it requires moving design authority to the people doing the work. Run the best workshop in the world and then redesign the output upstairs, and you haven't done co-design — you've done a more elaborate form of telling people what to build, while spending their goodwill in the process. As Lewin put it, people can't be trained for democracy by autocratic means.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — move design authority to the people doing the work, don't simulate it.**
- Give the **self-managing team (DP2)** real authority to design its own work, so the workshop output *is* the decision, not an input to one made elsewhere.
- Decide before the session what's genuinely on the table; if the design will be reworked above, say so — don't stage participation over a foregone conclusion.
- Judge participative methods by whether they transfer authority, not by how collaborative the room felt.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Be honest about scope up front: "this is consultation, here's what's fixed and what's open." Named consultation keeps trust; disguised consultation burns it.
- Carry as much of the workshop's actual output through to the final design as your authority allows, and show people where their input landed.
- Notice authority's effect in the room — your presence as facilitator or manager is already shaping behaviour; minimise it where you can.
