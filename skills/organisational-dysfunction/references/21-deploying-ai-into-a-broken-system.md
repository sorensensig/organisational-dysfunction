# Deploying AI into a broken system

*Dysfunction #21 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7455917294859284480-owYr) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- An AI rollout starts with promise, then adoption goes ragged: some people lean in, others quietly route around it.
- Concerns surface — deskilling, loss of judgement, who's accountable when the tool is wrong — and get waved away as "resistance to change."
- The tool slowly mutates from assistant into monitor: tracking output, flagging the slow, justifying who gets cut.
- The expected productivity jump doesn't materialise, or it shows up as more surveillance and less trust rather than better work.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

This is the oldest sociotechnical mistake in a new costume. For decades, organisations have introduced technology with enormous attention to technical capability and almost none to the social system it lands in — and the social system is what determines whether the technology helps or harms. We're in the fourth industrial revolution making exactly the mistake of the third.

The outcome is decided by the structure the AI drops into. In a **DP1** design — one person, one job, one replaceable part — automation logically points at the human: if the task can be done by a tool, the person holding that fragment becomes redundant *by design*. So AI naturally becomes a control-and-cost mechanism, and the workforce is right to be wary. In a **DP2** design, teams own a whole piece of work along with the judgement, context and adaptability no tool replicates; there, AI becomes an extension of the group's capability rather than a replacement for its parts. Same technology, opposite consequences — the difference is entirely structural.

So the real question is never "is the AI good?" It's "what is the AI good *for* in this structure?" Deploy it into a fragmented, control-oriented system and it amplifies fragmentation and control. The system was broken before the tool arrived; the tool just runs the breakage faster.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — fix the system before you scale the tool into it.**
- Move ownership of whole tasks to **self-managing teams (DP2)** first, so AI augments a group that holds context rather than picking off isolated, replaceable steps.
- Let the people doing the work decide where AI helps and where it doesn't — they can see where judgement is irreplaceable.
- Design the tool as an extension of human capability, not a meter on human output. If it's measuring people, it's already in the wrong structure.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Treat "resistance" as data, not defiance — when people avoid a tool, they're usually telling you something true about how it changes their work or their standing.
- Pilot with a team that owns its whole workflow, and keep the tool's outputs in the team's hands, not the dashboard above it.
- Refuse, where you can, to wire AI metrics into headcount or performance decisions — that's the move that turns an assistant into a threat.
