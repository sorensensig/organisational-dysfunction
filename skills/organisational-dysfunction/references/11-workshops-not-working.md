# Workshops not working

*Dysfunction #11 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7450423358992793600-JiZK) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The workshop was carefully planned and well facilitated, yet it felt forced, flat, and like a waste of everyone's afternoon.
- Participants show up because they're told to, then sit on their hands, defer, or quietly resist.
- The facilitator is also the boss — so the "exercise" feels like a route to a conclusion that's already been decided.
- Output is thin or politely agreeable, and nobody owns what came out of the room.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The usual postmortem blames the people ("low energy", "not bought in") or the technique ("wrong format"). The real fault line is who owns what. A facilitator's legitimate job is to hold the *process*; the moment they also own the decision to run the workshop, the choice of technique, or — worst — the *content* and its conclusions, they've collapsed two roles that must stay separate.

When the person steering the room also holds positional power over the content, you're no longer in a workshop, you're in a meeting wearing a workshop costume. That structure reliably triggers Bion's basic-assumption behaviour: dependency (wait to be told), fight/flight (resist or disengage), or factionalism. None of these are character flaws — they're the predictable group response to control dressed up as participation. This is DP1 logic (the leader integrates and decides) smuggled into a setting that only works under DP2 (the group owns its own work).

So poor workshops are a structural signal, not a participation deficit. You cannot facilitate your way out of having the wrong person own the content.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — split facilitation from authority so the group genuinely owns the output.**
- Let the facilitator manage *only* the process; participants own the content, the conclusions, and the output.
- Keep people with positional power over the topic out of the facilitator seat — bring in someone with no stake in the answer.
- Have the group itself shape why it's meeting and what counts as a good result, rather than receiving a pre-set agenda. This is DP2 instead of DP1.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- If you must facilitate your own team, say out loud what's decided and what's genuinely open — don't fake a choice that isn't there.
- Hand the pen over: let the group set the question and capture its own conclusions, so ownership stays with them.
- Read passivity as a signal about power in the room, not as apathy to be "energised" away.
