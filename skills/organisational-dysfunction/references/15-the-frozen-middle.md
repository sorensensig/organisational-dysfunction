# The frozen middle

*Dysfunction #15 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7451962689595019264-Bivr) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- After an agile transformation, teams have "autonomy" — yet decisions get stuck, information stops flowing, and initiatives die quietly with no explanation.
- Middle managers are perpetually busy in meetings, but nothing seems to move through them.
- Leadership concludes the middle layer is "resisting" or "not getting it," and reaches for training, mindset work, or replacing people.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

This is the signature failure of a **half-finished redesign**. Teams have been pushed toward DP2 (self-management) while the surrounding organisation is still DP1 (hierarchical). In that gap, middle managers lose their old function — pushing work down and reporting status up — but are given no genuine new one.

The result is not deliberate obstruction. It's structural: the middle becomes a **dampening layer** that, often unconsciously, preserves the existing power structure simply because the old wiring still runs through it. Approvals, budgets, prioritisation, and information still flow through roles whose reason to exist has been removed. Blaming the individuals misreads a structural vacuum as a character or competence problem.

## What to do

**The real fix is to finish the transition, not to fix the managers.**
- In a coherent DP2 organisation, coordination is done **by the self-managing teams themselves, peer-to-peer** — not via managerial proxies. So the question is not "how do we get the middle to enable the teams?" but "what coordination work genuinely needs doing, and can the teams own it directly?"
- Redesign the whole system, not a layer of it. Move decision rights, budget authority, and information flows to where the work happens, and explicitly redefine (or dissolve) middle roles rather than leaving them as orphaned bottlenecks.
- Give former managers a real new function — many are well placed to work on the *system* (boundaries, capability, removing structural impediments, connecting teams to their environment) rather than routing work.

**If you can't change the whole structure yet:**
- Name the trap honestly: the friction is a predicted symptom of a partial transformation, not the middle "failing." This reframing alone defuses a lot of misdirected blame.
- Find the specific decisions and flows still routed through the middle and move them, one concrete handoff at a time, to the teams.
