# It's just a job

*Dysfunction #48 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7472171275948544000-RSWR) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- People describe their work as "just a job" — said as a boundary, but masking apathy and quiet exhaustion.
- Fatigue that doesn't lift with rest; energy gone before the day even ends.
- The short temper and withdrawal at work follow people home — snapping at family, going quiet about what they do all day.
- Over time, the erosion is physical and mental health, not just morale. The damage spills past the office walls.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

Drawing on Merrelyn Emery's open-systems analysis of bureaucratic dysfunction, the key is that this is a *spiral*, not a stable state. A DP1 structure — control held above the work, individuals each accountable for a fragment — reliably produces a particular emotional yield: contempt, humiliation, guilt, shame, anger. Those feelings aren't incidental friction; they're the structure's output when people are denied control over work they're held responsible for.

People then do the only rational thing: they defend themselves. They withdraw, form cliques, play politics, pass the buck. But each of those defences degrades the system a little more, which generates more distress, which calls for more defence. The loop tightens over time. "It's just a job" is what self-protective withdrawal sounds like once it has hardened — the affect of someone who has stopped expecting the work to give anything back.

This is the crucial reframe: it is not a collection of individual failures, weak resilience, or bad attitudes. It is the predictable, structural output of DP1 applied at scale and sustained over years. And because the spiral runs through the whole person, the cost doesn't stay at work — apathy and exhaustion on the job feed disruption at home and in the community. The system is exporting its dysfunction into people's lives.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — give people control over their own work, which is what stops the spiral generating distress.**
- Move coordination and decision rights to the people doing the work (DP2), so responsibility is matched with the authority to act on it.
- Redesign whole tasks people can own, replacing fragmented accountability with meaningful control — the condition under which the negative-affect spiral cannot start.
- Recognise that wellbeing programmes bolted onto a DP1 structure treat the symptom; the source is the design that keeps producing the distress.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Name the spiral honestly with peers: the withdrawal and politics aren't character flaws, they're defences against a structure that gives no control. Seeing the mechanism reduces the self-blame.
- Protect the boundary deliberately so the spillover into home and health is contained while the structural fight is fought.
- Carve out whatever local pockets of genuine ownership you can — small areas where you actually control the work — since control is the antidote, even in miniature.
