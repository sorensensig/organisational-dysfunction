# The Sunday email

*Dysfunction #34 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7464920253190426624--Gbh) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- A leader fires off a non-urgent message at 21:47 on a Sunday, with a sincere "no need to reply tonight."
- People reply anyway — and quickly. The disclaimer is read as the cover, the timestamp as the real instruction.
- Over time, the people who advance are reliably the always-available ones.
- The handbook promises work-life balance; the lived environment rewards the opposite.
- Nobody mandated overwork. It propagates by example, one Sunday email at a time.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

Modelled behaviour always beats stated values. People navigate by reading the environment, not the policy document — so when the senior signal and the written value disagree, the signal wins. The Sunday email *is* the environment, and it teaches availability as the price of belonging.

But the leader sending it usually isn't a villain; they're caught in the same trap. Under DP1, individuals are held personally accountable for outcomes they can't actually control alone. That mismatch — total responsibility, partial control — generates chronic, structural anxiety at every level of the hierarchy. Performative overwork is what that anxiety does for a living: if you can't guarantee the outcome, you can at least be seen to be always on. The Sunday email is a symptom of a person trying to manage an impossible accountability by being ever-present.

So a policy banning out-of-hours email treats the rash and leaves the infection. The structural need to send it is still there. Address the message and the anxiety simply finds another channel.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — distribute responsibility to the group so no individual carries an accountability they can't meet alone.**
- Move ownership of outcomes onto self-managing teams, so no single person is left personally answerable for what only a group can deliver.
- When responsibility is genuinely shared, the chronic anxiety that drives performative availability has nothing to feed on.
- Make work-life balance a *result* of the design — a load no one person has to absorb — not a value defended by willpower.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Leaders: change what you *model*, not just what you say — schedule-send to working hours; your timestamps are louder than your handbook.
- Make it safe and normal not to reply, by visibly not replying yourself and never rewarding those who do.
- Name the trap kindly: the over-availability is the structure's anxiety talking, not the individual's failing — and don't promote on the basis of it.
