# Forming–storming–norming–performing

*Dysfunction #5 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7447891509602930688-wFJG) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Tuckman's four-stage model is invoked as an iron law: every new team *must* "storm" before it can perform.
- Early conflict and jockeying are treated as a healthy, mandatory rite of passage rather than a warning sign.
- Leaders settle in to "manage the team through storming," expecting a protracted period of friction.
- A drawn-out, painful norming phase is normalised — "this is just what new teams go through."
- Real productivity is postponed for weeks or months while everyone waits for the cohesion to arrive.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

Tuckman's sequence describes what happens to a particular *kind* of team: one assembled and run from above. It only makes sense inside an autocratic bureaucracy — a DP1 structure built on Theory-X assumptions that people must be closely directed and will fight over who's in charge. The storming is real, but it's manufactured by the design: hand a group no shared authority, no jointly owned task, and an ambiguous pecking order, and of course they'll spend their first weeks fighting it out.

The error is treating that as universal human team behaviour rather than an artefact of one organisational form. The dysfunction here isn't the model's accuracy; it's elevating a description of DP1 dynamics into a prescription everyone should endure — and then designing teams to guarantee the very storming the model predicts.

Build the team differently and the sequence changes. A self-managing group that has designed its own working arrangements through Participative Design starts with shared ownership of goals and method already in place. There's far less to fight over, so it can jump from forming more or less straight to performing. Structure determines the dynamics, not the other way round.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — design the team for DP2 so the storming has nothing to feed on.**
- Stand teams up as self-managing groups with a whole, jointly owned piece of work and the authority to run it.
- Let the team design its own roles, norms, and coordination — Participative Design — rather than having them imposed and then contested.
- Build on Theory-Y assumptions: people are capable of self-direction, so you don't need a phase that exists mainly to settle who controls whom.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Don't valorise storming or plan for it as inevitable — treat early conflict as a signal that ownership and goals are unclear, and clarify those.
- Get the team to agree its own working agreements early and explicitly; much "norming" friction is just unstated expectations colliding.
- Reframe for leaders: a smooth start isn't a team "skipping a stage," it's evidence the design is sound. The painful version is the symptom, not the standard.
