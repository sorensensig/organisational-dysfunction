# Team leads

*Dysfunction #9 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7448374766131990528-6gmd) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- A team is told to self-organise, and also given a formal "team lead" — and nobody is sure who actually decides.
- The lead adopts "servant leadership" or "situational leadership" to seem non-directive, yet the positional power is still there and everyone feels it.
- Decisions wobble between "the team agreed" and "well, the lead signed off," depending on who you ask.
- People route choices through the lead out of habit, undercutting the autonomy the team was supposedly granted.
- Coordination and accountability get murky precisely because two contradictory operating models are live at once.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

This is two design principles bolted together that genuinely cannot mix. A DP1 organisation *needs* personal leadership positions — that's the mechanism through which control, coordination, and alignment happen; authority lives in roles above the work. Agile pushes the other way, toward DP2, where the team itself holds those functions and coordinates peer-to-peer. The "team lead" sits exactly on the fault line between them.

Hjorteland's image is oil and water: DP1 and DP2 are so fundamentally different that no amount of clever adaptation — servant, situational, coaching, "first among equals" — dissolves the contradiction. The fancy leadership style is an attempt to *behave* like DP2 while *occupying* a DP1 position of power. The result is permanent ambiguity about who really decides, because structurally two answers are true at once. The confusion isn't a maturity gap or a styling problem to be coached away; it's baked into running both paradigms simultaneously. And half a transformation here is often worse than none — you get the overhead of hierarchy and the uncertainty of self-management together.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — stop adapting incompatible models; commit to DP2 and let the lead position go.**
- Ditch the formal team-lead role and put authority with teams everywhere: each team owns its goals and handles cross-team integration peer-to-peer.
- Make leadership a *function* the team can assign for a specific need or moment, not a standing position of power over peers.
- Run it as participative democracy throughout — coordination by negotiation between teams, not by a layer of personal authority above them.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Make the ambiguity explicit: write down exactly which decisions are the team's and which are the lead's, so the contradiction stops hiding in "it depends."
- Have the lead deliberately hand decisions back to the team and default to facilitating rather than deciding — narrowing, in practice, the DP1 footprint.
- Reframe for the team: the confusion isn't anyone failing at their job, it's two systems colliding by design. Naming that lifts the blame off individuals and points at the real fix.
