# The collaboration that isn't

*Dysfunction `#32` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7463121890333720576-Ff17) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Two teams are told to "collaborate" on something, and it opens with genuine goodwill.
- Over weeks the energy curdles: credit lands unevenly, decisions drift toward one side, the other side goes quiet.
- One team is quietly setting the terms; the other is increasingly just complying.
- Nobody can point to where it went wrong — there was no row, the collaboration just hollowed out.
- It continues in name on the status report, but the spirit has left.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

Two separate social systems were asked to work as one without anyone building the one. They assumed a shared purpose instead of agreeing it, assumed coordination would emerge, and left the boundary between them undefined. "Collaboration" was named as a value, not designed as a structure.

A structural vacuum doesn't stay empty. With no agreed purpose, no explicit coordination mechanism and no jointly-owned rules, the space gets filled by the existing power gradient — DP1 by default. The team with more leverage starts, without malice, setting the terms; the other adapts. What looks like laissez-faire freedom is actually domination with the paperwork left blank. The drift to one-sidedness isn't a relationship failure; it's what *always* happens when you put two systems together and specify nothing.

So the goodwill was never the missing ingredient. Goodwill is what you spend down in the absence of design. The choice the organisation skipped is the real one: *what kind* of cross-boundary relationship is this supposed to be?

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — pick a relationship type and actually design it; don't outsource the boundary to goodwill.**
- If the work is arm's-length, make it **transactional**: an explicit contract, clear deliverables, defined interfaces — clean handoffs, no pretence of fusion.
- If it genuinely needs to be one system, make it **systemic**: a mutually agreed purpose, an explicit coordination structure, and design principles both sides own together.
- Decide *which* one on purpose. The failure mode is choosing neither and calling it collaboration.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Name the missing agreement out loud — "what's our shared goal, and who decides what?" — before more goodwill burns off.
- Make the implicit power gradient visible; you can't renegotiate terms nobody admits are being set.
- Stop framing the drift as the other team being difficult; it's the predictable result of an undesigned boundary.
