# The pilot trap

*Dysfunction #42 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7468542180836519936-YvZu) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- A new way of working is trialled with one pilot team. It goes well; the people in it are energised and productive.
- When the question turns to scaling, resistance appears — and it's strongest from those closest to the experiment: managers of the teams that weren't picked, HR, architects, the steering committee.
- The pilot is either wound down or quietly allowed to drift back to the old way of working.
- Leadership concludes the approach "works in theory but isn't right for us."
- The very success of the pilot seems to generate the forces that kill it.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The pilot trap is not a failure of the pilot. The pilot usually works on its merits — that's the point. What fails is the surrounding system's tolerance for it. A partial patch of self-managing DP2 embedded inside a bureaucratic DP1 organisation is inherently unstable, because the two operate on incompatible distributions of authority. The pilot is a visible, structural provocation to everyone not inside it: it implies their way of working is the problem. That triggers the organisation's immune response, and it absorbs or expels the foreign tissue.

Merrelyn Emery's notion of *paradoxical inhibition* (from Pavlov's conditioning work) names the cruel twist: those structurally closest to the change feel the most threatened and develop the strongest resistance. The unselected manager, the architect whose authority the pilot bypasses, the HR process the pilot ignores — these aren't irrational obstructionists. They are accurately perceiving that the new model, if it spread, would dissolve the basis of their roles. Their resistance is rational. The fence between DP1 and DP2 will hold, and it holds from the DP1 side.

This is the deeper reason "let's just try it small and scale what works" so reliably disappoints. You cannot introduce DP2 incrementally into a DP1 structure; the incompatibility isn't softened by a smaller dose, it's concentrated.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — change the whole system at once, because you can't grow DP2 inside DP1 one team at a time.**
- Don't pilot. Use whole-system methods — Search Conferences, Participative Design Workshops — that redesign the surrounding structure simultaneously, not one island within it.
- Bring the threatened roles (managers, HR, architecture, governance) *into* the redesign as participants, so the change happens *with* them rather than *to* them.
- Redesign the authority structure itself, not just one team's practices, so there's no DP1 fence left for the new model to break against.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Be honest that a lone pilot inside an unchanged structure is likely to be reabsorbed — set expectations accordingly rather than promising scale.
- Use the pilot to build a coalition and surface the structural constraints, not as proof you can scale by repetition.
- Reframe the resistance: the people fighting it aren't backward — they're correctly reading a threat to roles the current design depends on.
