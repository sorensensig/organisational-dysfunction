# The corridor conversation

*Dysfunction #59 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7477598111603859456-lBUO) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Meetings run smoothly with nothing challenged, then the real opinions come out in the corridor or on Slack afterwards.
- The people with the operational knowledge stay silent in the room where the decision is made.
- Decision-makers act on incomplete information because the people who knew better said nothing.
- "That was never going to work, everyone knew" — voiced only after the fact, never in front of the person who could have changed course.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

In a DP1 structure, communication is asymmetrical by design: orders flow down and responses flow up, with no real conversation between equals. Bion's basic-assumption *dependency* takes hold — the group orients around the authority figure rather than the task. Because subordinates' careers depend on their superiors, the rational move is to manage upward: agree, defer, smooth things over, avoid challenging. Do this long enough and it stops being a conscious calculation and becomes an unconscious habit.

So the corridor conversation isn't a courage problem or a culture problem — it's the predictable output of an authority gradient. People aren't withholding their views because they're timid; they're reading a structure in which candour to power carries personal risk and little upside, and routing their honesty to where it's safe. The truth still flows, just not into the room where it would matter.

This is why "we need more psychological safety" or "please speak up" rarely fixes it. You're asking people to behave as peers inside a structure that makes them dependents. The silence is structurally smart. Change the gradient or the silence stays.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — dissolve the authority gradient so communication becomes negotiation between peers.**
- Move toward DP2, where coordination happens among equals and all communication is a negotiation between peers rather than orders-down/responses-up.
- Make the goal genuinely shared and accountability mutual, so candour serves a common task instead of risking a personal relationship with a boss.
- Put the people with operational knowledge in real decision-making roles, not advisory ones — give their view structural weight, not just airtime.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Treat the corridor as data: the real conversation is happening, just not where you can act on it — go find it and bring it back to the table.
- Lower your own gradient deliberately — ask for dissent by name, react well when you get it, and never punish the messenger.
- Reframe the silence as a rational response to the authority structure, not a sign your people lack courage or buy-in.
