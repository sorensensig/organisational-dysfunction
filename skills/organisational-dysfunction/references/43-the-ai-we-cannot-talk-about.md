# The AI we cannot talk about

*Dysfunction #43 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7469628117905625089-71F3) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The organisation publicly champions "responsible AI" and sustainability commitments, and means them — on paper.
- At an industry panel, an audience question about the environmental cost of these systems goes unanswered.
- The reason is structural: vendor NDAs prevent the people who know from disclosing what they know.
- A facade of transparency sits over a systematic, contractually enforced silence about impacts that are well understood internally.
- Everyone in the room senses the gap between what's said and what can be said.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

This dysfunction isn't a shortage of information — the relevant facts exist and are known inside the firms. It's a structural block on information *flow*. NDAs are precisely engineered to stop knowledge circulating across the field, and that circulation is exactly what an industry needs to adapt. For a field in motion — a turbulent environment where conditions shift faster than any single actor can track — Fred Emery argued that the only viable coordinating mechanism is *shared values*. Shared values can't form without shared knowledge. Cut the knowledge flow and you sever the field's capacity for self-correction.

The pattern echoes the oil industry: internal knowledge, external silence, and the result is a whole sector unable to adapt because the signals that should reach it are contractually suppressed. This is a boundary problem one level up from the single organisation — the field's perceptual apparatus has been deliberately blinded. Each firm optimises its own position (protect the vendor relationship, honour the NDA) while collectively the industry loses the ability to read its own environment and develop the shared values that would let it respond.

So "responsible AI" statements aren't hypocrisy in the ordinary sense. They're what's left when the conditions for genuine collective adaptation — open circulation of what's actually known — have been contractually removed.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — restore knowledge flow across the field so shared values can form.**
- Break the contractual silencing: refuse, renegotiate, or collectively resist NDA terms that suppress environmental and impact data.
- Treat field-level transparency as a precondition for "responsible" anything — without circulating knowledge, the label is empty.
- Build mechanisms (industry bodies, open reporting standards) through which the sector can perceive its own impact and coordinate on shared values.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Surface and document internally what can't be said externally, so the organisation at least retains its own honest picture.
- Push, where you have standing, for disclosure terms in vendor contracts rather than accepting boilerplate silence.
- Reframe the discomfort: the panellist's non-answer isn't evasiveness or bad faith — it's a contract speaking through a person who has been structurally gagged.
