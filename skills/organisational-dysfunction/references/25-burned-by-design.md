# Burned by design

*Dysfunction `#25` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7459870386411778048-wTtP) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Burnout is widespread but framed as temporary — a capacity blip to be managed with better prioritisation, fewer meetings, and firmer boundaries.
- Remedies are individual: resilience workshops, mindfulness apps, encouragement to "switch off." Some people recover; others quietly leave.
- A subtler stage precedes it — presenteeism, people physically present but mentally checked out — and it's mistaken for coping rather than read as a warning.
- New hires arrive fresh and, within a year or two, show the exact same pattern. The faces change; the burnout doesn't.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

Burnout here is not primarily a resilience or capacity problem — it's a **job-design** problem. The recurring drivers are structural features of **DP1**: lack of control over one's own work, demands that are unclear or contradictory, and the absence of meaningful feedback. Those three conditions reliably exhaust people regardless of how robust they are, because the human is being asked to absorb the strain of a structure that gives them responsibility without authority.

That the same pattern reappears in each new occupant is the tell. If it were about individual resilience, fresh people wouldn't predictably burn out in the same roles. The role is doing it. Adding support resources helps individuals *tolerate* a harmful design a little longer; it doesn't remove the harm. As the metaphor goes, the tree was planted in the wrong soil — repotting isn't optional, and no amount of watering substitutes for it.

The evidence is that the structural change is the effective intervention, not the wellness programme. A documented DP1-to-DP2 transition produced a 28% drop in absenteeism and an 81% rise in engagement in the first year — with no major technical change. The variable was the design of the work, not the grit of the workers.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — repot the work, don't just water the people.**
- Redesign roles toward **DP2**: give teams real control over their own work, clear and coherent demands, and direct feedback from the outcome.
- Treat presenteeism and disengagement as early structural alarms, not as individual failings to be motivated away.
- Measure whether the *conditions* are improving (control, clarity, feedback) rather than whether people are "coping" better.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Where you have any local authority, give your team more say over how its work is done — even partial control eases the strain.
- Make demands explicit and close feedback loops within your reach; ambiguity and silence are two of the three drivers.
- Name it honestly: this is a design problem, not a personal weakness. That reframing protects people from carrying blame that belongs to the structure.
