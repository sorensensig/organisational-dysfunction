# Fixing people

*Dysfunction #22 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7455917663668621312-IAIZ) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Someone is disengaged, missing delivery, or in conflict — and the response is aimed squarely at the individual: a performance improvement plan, a coaching engagement, a personality assessment.
- The intervention "works" for a while, then fades. Or the person leaves, a replacement arrives, and the same trouble reappears in the same seat.
- The role has a reputation: "that position chews people up." Yet each new occupant is treated as a fresh personal case.
- HR and managers accumulate a thick file of individual remedies and a thin record of anything actually changing.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

When the *same* problem keeps appearing across *different* people in the *same* role, the problem is not those people — it's what the structure does to whoever sits there. Personalising a structural defect is a category error, and an expensive one.

A **DP1** design manufactures the very behaviours it then tries to coach away. It makes people compete for recognition (because credit is scarce and individuated) and dependent on management for decisions (because authority sits one level above the work). Those two features reliably produce disengagement, defensiveness, and avoidance — not because of who's in the role, but because that's the rational adaptation to being held responsible for work you don't control. The coaching targets the symptom; the structure keeps generating it.

There's a deeper trap here. "Fixing the person" doesn't just fail to solve the dysfunction — it *legitimises* it. Every individual intervention quietly certifies that the structure is fine and the human is the fault. So the design is protected from scrutiny precisely by the effort spent rescuing the people it breaks.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — change the question from "how do we fix this person?" to "what is the structure doing to whoever holds this role?"**
- When a problem recurs across occupants, treat the *position* as the unit of diagnosis, not the person. Look at the authority, ownership, and feedback built into that seat.
- Move toward **DP2**: give the role real control over its own work and the coordination around it, so the conditions that produce disengagement aren't there to coach away.
- Spend the coaching budget on redesigning the conditions, not on retrofitting the human to survive them.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Before launching a PIP, ask "has this happened to others in this role?" If yes, you're looking at a design fault wearing a person's name.
- Protect the individual from misdirected blame — name the structural pattern out loud so the file reads "the role is broken," not "the person is."
- Fix what you locally can about the seat's conditions (decision rights, clarity of demand, feedback), even if the wider structure stays put.
