# Passing the buck

*Dysfunction `#2` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7447224524368052225-Qlxj) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- A team is ready to make a call about its own work, then stops to "check with the grown-ups first."
- Decisions are kicked upstairs even when nobody upstairs knows the work better than the team does.
- People hedge and qualify rather than commit: "we could do X, but management would have to sign off."
- Nobody quite owns the outcome — when it goes wrong, responsibility evaporates between the team and the layer above.
- Even small, reversible choices stall waiting for a permission that may never explicitly arrive.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

This is the friction of a team that *looks* self-managing but has never been granted the authority to act like it. They behave as if DP2 applies — they expect to own their work — yet the moment a decision carries weight, they discover the authority still lives a level above them. So they defer.

The dangerous part is that the structure is neither honest DP1 nor honest DP2. It's a laissez-faire muddle: nobody has clearly said who decides what. Hjorteland is blunt that this ambiguity is *worse* than command-and-control, because at least DP1 is predictable — you know who to ask and what will happen. Here, authority is undefined, so deferring upward is the only rational move. The team isn't being timid out of character weakness; it is reading a structure that punishes uninvited initiative and protecting itself accordingly.

Passing the buck, then, is a symptom of missing design, not missing courage. Telling people to "just take ownership" inside an undefined authority structure simply asks them to gamble with consequences they don't control.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — stop leaving authority undefined; name where each decision actually sits.**
- Make decision rights explicit: list the classes of decisions the team genuinely owns, end to end, and put real accountability with that ownership.
- If you mean for them to self-manage, grant the authority *and* the consequences together (DP2) — autonomy without accountability is just the same ambiguity rebranded.
- If the work genuinely needs central control, say so clearly (DP1) — a predictable hierarchy beats a fog where everyone hedges.
- Remove the silent veto: if a manager can quietly overturn the team's calls, the team will keep deferring no matter what the org chart claims.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Surface the ambiguity directly — ask leadership, in writing, which decisions are the team's to make. Forcing the question often exposes that the "permission" was never required.
- Default to acting on reversible decisions and informing afterward, rather than asking first; build a track record that earns wider latitude.
- Reframe with the team: their caution is a sane response to undefined authority, not a failure of nerve. The fix is clarity, not bravado.
