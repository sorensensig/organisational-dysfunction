# The pair that runs everything

*Dysfunction #49 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7472585057053069314-CLCO) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Two people have quietly become the team's coordination hub — everyone defers to them, their PRs sail through, the real design decisions happen in a thread between the two of them.
- The rest of the team orbits: present in standups, but spectators to the actual choices.
- From the outside the team looks high-performing and stable.
- Then one of the pair leaves, and within a year the team's coordination falls apart — because there was never any coordination structure other than the pair.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

This is Bion's pairing assumption playing out at team level. When a group has no genuine shared structure for coordinating its own work, two competent people will naturally fill the vacuum and become a surrogate hub. There's a regenerative version of pairing — creative partnership that lifts the whole group — but what shows up in most agile teams is the destructive form: asymmetrical relations with everyone else, parallel monologues instead of group conversation, and a stability that is actually brittle.

The root cause is structural, and it's easy to miss because the symptom looks like success. A team left as a laissez-faire collection of individuals — given the *label* of a self-managing team but never the *structure* of one — has no mechanism for the group as a whole to set direction and coordinate. Nature abhors that vacuum. The pairing hardens into dependency: the two carry the coordination the structure failed to provide, and everyone else's involvement atrophies because there's no shared channel for it.

So the high performance is borrowed, not built. It rests on two people, not on a structure the team owns. When the pair breaks, there's nothing underneath. The structure failed the team, and the pair was the price it paid to look healthy in the meantime.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — give the team a genuine shared coordination structure so it doesn't have to outsource coordination to two people.**
- Build real group-level coordination: the whole team setting goals, making design decisions in the open, and owning the task together — DP2 in substance, not just in name.
- Distribute the work the pair has been silently absorbing — decision-making, integration, context-holding — across the team as a shared function.
- Stop reading "two strong people carry everything" as success; treat it as a signal that the coordination structure is missing.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Make the pair's hidden work visible: surface the design decisions into group conversation instead of private threads, so others can participate.
- Build redundancy deliberately — rotate who leads coordination, spread context, so the team isn't one resignation away from collapse.
- Reframe it for the team and the pair: this isn't two people hogging control or everyone else being passive; it's a vacuum the structure created and the pair rationally filled.
