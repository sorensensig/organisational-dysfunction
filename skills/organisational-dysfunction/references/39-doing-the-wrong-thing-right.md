# Doing the wrong thing right

*Dysfunction #39 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7467458754603720706-XwKL) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The organisation runs a constant stream of improvement programmes — efficiency drives, restructures, culture initiatives — and gets measurably better at what it already does.
- Meanwhile, signals from the environment — a new technology, a regulatory shift, a competitor's move — are raised by frontline people and processed slowly, watered down, or ignored.
- Dashboards are green, internal metrics improve, and yet the organisation feels increasingly out of step with its market.
- The gap between the organisation and its environment widens quietly, without any single visible failure to point at.
- Everyone is busy and competent; the work is being done well. It's just the wrong work.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

Open systems stay alive by managing two things at once: how well they function internally, *and* how well they stay aligned with a moving environment. This dysfunction is what happens when an organisation pours all its attention into the first and neglects the second. It's Drucker's distinction made structural — efficiency (doing things right) is being optimised while effectiveness (doing the right thing) is being lost. You cannot tune your way into relevance.

The structural culprit is DP1. By concentrating perception and decision-making at the top, bureaucracy forces the whole organisation to see its environment through a single narrow aperture. Emery and Trist's "turbulent field" (the Type IV environment, where the ground itself is moving) is exactly the condition where that narrow aperture is fatal: the environment changes faster than a single apex can perceive and respond, so the people at the boundary who *do* feel the change have nowhere to put the signal. Misalignment then arrives not through internal failure but through disconnection — the organisation gets better and better at yesterday's task.

So relentless internal improvement isn't a virtue here; it's a symptom. It's the visible activity of a system that has lost the ability to ask whether the task itself is still the right one.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — distribute perception so the whole system, not just the top, stays coupled to its environment.**
- Move toward DP2: every group at the boundary should be able to import an environmental signal and act on it, not just escalate it.
- Design for effectiveness before efficiency — make "is this still the right task?" a standing question owned where the work meets the world.
- Treat boundary-spanning (contact with users, market, technology) as core structural work, not a side activity for a strategy team.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Create direct, fast channels from frontline signal to whoever holds direction-setting power, and protect them from being filtered to mush.
- Make the cost of ignored signals legible — track the environmental shifts that were flagged and dropped.
- Reframe the conversation: green internal metrics are not evidence of health if the task they measure is going stale.
