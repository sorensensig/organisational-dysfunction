# Outsourcing the future

*Dysfunction `#37` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7466024296906301440-IKpO) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Leadership hires a consultancy to "do the strategy" because they lack the bandwidth, the confidence, or the appetite to do it themselves.
- Months of interviews, benchmarking, and offsite workshops produce a polished deck that lands with a thud of authority.
- Implementation immediately hits questions the document never anticipated; the people doing the work weren't in the room and don't recognise the picture.
- The consultants have moved on by the time the hard problems surface, so there's no one left who can adapt the plan.
- Two years later the strategy is quietly shelved and a new firm is hired to write the next one. The cycle repeats.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

This is a participation problem dressed up as a strategy problem. A strategy isn't a document — it's a living, shared understanding of where the system is, where it needs to go, and why. That understanding is distributed across the people who run the operation, the relationships they hold, and the environment they transact with every day. An outside firm, however skilled, cannot import knowledge that only exists *inside* the system. Interviewing a representative sample extracts fragments and strips out the tacit, relational knowing that makes a strategy actually executable.

Structurally this is DP1 thinking applied to direction-setting: perception and decision-making are concentrated at the top (and then rented out to a vendor), one level — or several — above the work. The organisation's own boundary-spanning capacity, the people who actually feel the environment shifting, is treated as a data source to be mined rather than as the locus where strategy should be formed. So you get a plan with no owner. When it meets reality and fails, nobody has the standing or the understanding to adapt it; it just gets replaced.

The result is an organisation that has outsourced not just the work of strategy but its capacity to *do* strategy — each cycle leaving it more dependent and less able to read its own future.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — strategy has to be made by the system that will live it, not handed to it.**
- Put the whole system in the room. Use participative methods (Search Conference and similar) where the people who run and feel the operation build the shared picture themselves.
- Treat the environment as something the organisation perceives directly through its own people at the boundary, not something a vendor reports back.
- Make ownership a design property: those who set the direction must also be those who adapt it when it meets reality.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- If consultants are unavoidable, use them to facilitate a participative process, not to author the answer — the output must be the organisation's, in its own words.
- Insist the people who will implement are co-authors, not interviewees; protect their time to be in the room.
- Reframe the recurring failure: it isn't that the last firm picked the wrong strategy, it's that a handed-down strategy can't be adapted by people who didn't build it.
