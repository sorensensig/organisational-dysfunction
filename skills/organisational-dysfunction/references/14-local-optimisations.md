# Local optimisations

*Dysfunction #14 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7451591475345215488-qf-M) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- A team wants to improve its own way of working — move to event-driven architecture, switch from Scrum to Kanban — and simply can't.
- The change depends on other teams who won't adjust, so it stalls at the boundary.
- Management blocks it on grounds of "wider system impact," and the team has no way to argue past that.
- Sensible local improvements pile up undone, and the team learns that owning its own process is a fiction.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

This is a parts-to-whole problem. A local optimisation is perfectly fine *if* it touches no one else — but parts are interconnected, so it rarely does. A change one team makes ripples into the teams it depends on and the teams that depend on it. That tension is real and has to be resolved somehow.

The DP1 way to resolve it is top-down guardrails: management draws boundaries and withholds permission, so that no part can move without central sign-off. This keeps the whole "safe" by denying the parts the authority to adapt — and it's the very thing that strangles the improvements that would benefit everyone. The autonomy the team was promised evaporates exactly where it would matter, because coordination has been pulled up a level instead of being held by the people doing the work.

The DP2 alternative isn't "let every team do whatever it wants" — that just trades central rigidity for local sub-optimisation. It's distributed design authority *with* an obligation: teams design their own work, and in return must ensure it aligns with the goals of the whole and coordinate directly with everyone affected. Authority and the duty to integrate sit together, at the level of the work.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — give teams design authority paired with the duty to align and coordinate.**
- Let teams design their own work, conditioned on demonstrable alignment with the goals of the whole system.
- Require them to coordinate directly with every affected party, rather than routing approval up through management.
- Replace top-down guardrails with shared goals, so "the whole" is a purpose the parts optimise toward, not a gate they're held behind.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Bring the affected teams into the room yourself and negotiate the change peer-to-peer before it reaches a manager's desk.
- Frame your proposal explicitly in terms of the system goal, not just your team's convenience — make the whole-benefit case.
- Read the block not as malice but as a structure that has nowhere to put cross-team coordination except "up."
