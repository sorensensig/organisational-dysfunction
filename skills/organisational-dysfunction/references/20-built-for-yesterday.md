# Built for yesterday

*Dysfunction #20 in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7455916133561483264-YGQ2) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The organisation works harder every year but adapts less. Effort is up; responsiveness is down.
- Strategic plans are obsolete by the time they're rolled out — the world moved while the document was being approved.
- Decisions pile up at the top, and the queue there is the bottleneck. By the time a market signal reaches someone allowed to act on it, the moment has passed.
- Leadership reads the slowness as a people problem — laziness, misalignment, lack of urgency — and pushes harder on the same machine.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

This isn't a motivation problem; it's an environment-mismatch problem. Emery and Trist's causal-texture work describes how the field an organisation lives in changes character over time. Today's field is **turbulent** — technology and competitors shift faster than any plan can keep up with. But most organisations are still structured for a calmer, more predictable world: a **DP1 hierarchy** where perception and decision-making are concentrated at the top, optimised for stable conditions where a few smart people could plan and the rest could execute.

In a turbulent field, centralising perception is exactly the wrong move. It builds the bottleneck into the design: the people who can *see* the change aren't allowed to *respond* to it, and the people allowed to respond can't see it in time. The organisation is slow not because anyone is failing, but because it was built for a world that no longer exists. AI only sharpens the mismatch — it raises the metabolic rate of the environment while the org's reflexes stay the same.

You cannot out-discipline a structural mismatch. More urgency applied to a centralised structure just exhausts people faster while the lag persists.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — distribute adaptive capacity to where the environment is actually sensed.**
- Move toward a **DP2** design where self-managing teams own a whole piece of work and can act on what they see without routing it up and back down.
- Push the authority to perceive *and* respond down to the same place — every team scanning its slice of the environment, adapting, and feeding learning back in.
- Replace big up-front plans with short cycles: sense, act, learn. Direction can stay central; response must be local.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Shorten the decision loop for the few decisions you control — pre-authorise responses to anticipated conditions so the team doesn't escalate every time.
- Name the bottleneck honestly: when something is slow, ask "where did this wait?" not "who dropped the ball?" The wait is almost always a queue at an approval gate.
- Reframe "we're behind" as "we're built for a slower world" — it points blame at the design, not the team.
