# Out of sight, out of sync

*Dysfunction `#47` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7471078871044526081-Ip9U) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The remote team's formal machinery runs fine — tickets move, ceremonies happen — but the human side is quietly fraying.
- Decisions that should take five minutes take three days; small misunderstandings pile up instead of getting cleared.
- Nobody noticed a teammate was struggling until it became a problem.
- An in-person week resets everything — and then it slowly drifts back. The team is formally self-managing but socially thin.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

Remote work does not break DP2. A distributed team can absolutely own a whole task and coordinate as peers. The catch is that DP2 runs on a substrate of social fabric — trust, shared context, the sense of being in the same field — and that fabric needs constant repair. Co-located teams repair it without noticing: the overheard comment, the read on someone's face, the unplanned corridor exchange. Asch's conditions for genuine communication — openness, psychological similarity, a shared field, and mutual trust — all get topped up automatically by proximity.

Remote weakens all four at once, and worse, it removes the *ambient* repair. The cues we use to sense whether someone is overloaded or quietly checked out are mostly non-verbal and invisible over video. So the maintenance that co-located teams do unconsciously, all day, simply stops happening — and nobody decides to stop it. The structure depletes social capital continuously and the usual replenishment is gone.

This reframes the in-person offsite. The reason it works and then fades is that it's an injection of social capital into a structure that drains it every day. A quarterly top-up cannot substitute for the daily, informal repair that distance removed. The dysfunction isn't that the team chose remote; it's that remote made invisible maintenance work visible and expensive, and most teams never staffed it.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — make the invisible social maintenance deliberate, resourced work.**
- Build explicit practices that replace what proximity used to provide for free: regular peer check-ins, generous shared context, deliberate surfacing of how people are actually doing.
- Treat in-person time as social-capital investment and schedule it against the depletion rate — not as a perk, but as required maintenance.
- Protect synchronous peer connection inside the team's own coordination, so DP2 has the trust and shared field it depends on.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- As a team, name the cost out loud: the drift isn't anyone slacking, it's the predictable decay of social fabric that distance no longer repairs for you.
- Make the ambient signals explicit — ask directly how people are doing, since you can no longer read it off the room.
- Reframe slow decisions and misunderstandings as a sign of depleted shared context, and rebuild that context on purpose rather than blaming individuals.
