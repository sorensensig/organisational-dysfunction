# HiPPOs and dungeon masters

*Dysfunction `#13` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7451319430934020096--pco) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Decisions go the way the Highest-Paid Person's Opinion goes, regardless of what the data or the people closest to the work say.
- Even with a flat formal structure, informal power — rank, seniority, salary, age, gender, who's loudest — quietly decides outcomes.
- A "dungeon master" steers the group toward a predetermined conclusion while keeping the appearance of an open process.
- Expertise in the room gets overridden; the people who actually know defer to the people who outrank them.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The instinct is to fix this with better meeting technique — silent writing, 1-2-4-all, round-robins — to give everyone an equal voice. Hjorteland's sharper point: those techniques can *reinforce* the problem. They treat the group as a set of isolated individuals each contributing a separate view, and when you foreground individual differences, the person with the most informal power dominates the merge. You've built a structure that surfaces rank rather than dissolving it.

The deeper issue is that these methods start from *what separates* people — competing positions to be reconciled — rather than from *what joins* them. Begin with individuals defending fragments and you get a contest, and contests are won by power, not evidence. This is DP1 thinking at the level of process: integration happens through whoever sits highest, even when no one named them the decider.

The alternative is to build community *before* problem-solving. Search Conference methodology opens not with positions but with shared goals and a common desirable future — establishing what unites the group first, so that when differences surface they're worked inside an already-shared frame rather than across a power gradient. Get common ground in place early and the HiPPO has far less to dominate.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — establish shared ground before you let differences into the room.**
- Open with the group's shared goals and wishes for the future, building a common identity before tackling any contested problem.
- When divisive positions surface, set them aside and pull the group back to what joins them, working from shared values outward.
- Use designs like the Search Conference that construct community first, instead of techniques that aggregate isolated individual opinions.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Name the dynamic out loud: surface that rank, not evidence, is steering the decision, so it can be questioned.
- Anchor discussions on shared goals early, so contributions are weighed against common purpose rather than against who said them.
- Get expertise on the record before the HiPPO speaks — sequence matters when power is in the room.
