# Fear of making decisions

*Dysfunction `#16` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7453767823392346112-wBNj) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Teams are told they're autonomous, yet good ideas get shelved because no one's sure they're "allowed."
- People hesitate to change their own tools, process, or technical architecture without explicit permission.
- Decisions stall on the worry that a change might ripple badly into the rest of the org — better to do nothing than to overstep.
- The team waits for a green light that never formally comes, and momentum quietly dies.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

This is the predictable side effect of a half-finished DP2 transition. The org has handed teams the *rhetoric* of self-management — "you're empowered, you decide" — but left the DP1 plumbing intact: approval gates, sign-off chains, and the lingering sense that someone above is the real decider. The team is caught between two structures and gets the benefits of neither.

That hybrid is genuinely the worst place to stand. Pure bureaucracy at least gives clear accountability — you know who decides and you escalate. Genuine self-organisation gives real adaptive freedom. The in-between state offers neither: no clear authority to appeal to, and no felt permission to act. Hesitation isn't timidity; it's the rational read of a system that says "decide" while still wired to punish overstepping. Half a transformation here is worse than none.

The missing piece isn't courage or a permission slip — it's a defined way to make decisions that doesn't route through hierarchy. Without one, "autonomy" is just ambiguity, and ambiguity in a still-hierarchical setting reliably produces paralysis.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — give teams a decision mechanism that doesn't depend on hierarchical approval.**
- Adopt the advice process: anyone can make any decision, provided they first seek advice from everyone meaningfully affected and from those with relevant expertise.
- Finish the DP2 transition rather than leaving teams stranded between two structures — remove the approval gates that contradict the "you're empowered" message.
- Make accountability explicit under self-management, so authority is felt, not just announced.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Run the advice process informally inside your team: consult the affected and the expert, then decide and document — don't wait for a sign-off that isn't coming.
- Make the smallest reversible version of the decision, so acting is cheap and the org learns it's safe.
- Reframe the hesitation for your team: it's a structural double-bind, not a lack of nerve.
