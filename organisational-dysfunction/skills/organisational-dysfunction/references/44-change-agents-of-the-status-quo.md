# Change agents of the status quo

*Dysfunction `#44` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7469991827279056896-Is3Q) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The agile transformation is stalling — frustration, slow delivery, a widening gap between the principles on the wall and daily reality.
- Coaches are brought in. Surface improvements follow: better ceremonies, smoother stand-ups, a more hopeful vocabulary.
- Within a year the fundamental problems are back, in slightly different clothes.
- The coaches have settled into a buffering role — absorbing the teams' frustration and translating it into something leadership finds palatable.
- Everyone is doing "agile," and nothing structural has moved.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The selection pressure is the whole story. Coaches are hired and retained by management, which means the ones who survive are the ones compatible with DP1 — the directive, top-down control structure that pays them. A coach who challenges the hierarchy directly loses the engagement. So over time the role is selected, not for changing the system, but for fitting it. The coach becomes a buffer: a shock absorber sitting between teams who feel the dysfunction and a leadership that needs not to.

What this produces is DP1 infrastructure wearing DP2 vocabulary — what Hjorteland calls laissez-faire. Leadership and newly-minted team leads use the language of empowerment and coaching to preserve the existing dominance hierarchy while appearing to dismantle it. The words point at self-management; the authority structure underneath them is untouched. As Weinberg put it, an organisation is perfectly designed to get the results it gets — and a system that keeps reproducing the same dysfunction is, at some level, designed to need the *appearance* of change more than the change itself. The buffer role is how it gets the appearance.

This is why repeated interventions feel like running to stand still. They're not failing despite the system's effort; they're being neutralised by the system's design. Goodhart lurks here too: once "doing agile" becomes the measured target, ceremonies improve while the thing they were meant to indicate — real devolved authority — quietly doesn't.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — redesign the authority hierarchy, because coaching can't out-talk the structure that selects the coaches.**
- Change the distribution of authority itself (toward DP2), rather than adding coaching roles inside an unchanged DP1.
- Give coaches a mandate to change structure, sponsored from where authority actually sits — or stop pretending the role can drive transformation.
- Make the target real devolved authority, not adoption of ceremonies, so the work can't be discharged by improving the rituals.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Name the buffering honestly: if a coach is mainly absorbing frustration, that's a structural role, not a coaching failure — surface it rather than working harder inside it.
- Find the points where authority *could* genuinely move and concentrate effort there, instead of spreading thin across cosmetic improvements.
- Reframe the recurring relapse: the problems return not because people are resistant but because the design was never asked to change.
