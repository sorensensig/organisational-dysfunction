# The performance review

*Dysfunction `#41` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7468179745680199680-9dg_) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The team collaborates well for most of the year — then review season arrives and the collaborative spirit evaporates.
- People quietly start positioning: documenting individual contributions, angling for visibility, competing for the same ratings and budget.
- Everyone remembers that their pay, career, and standing are decided not by how the team performed but by how they personally look to the manager above them.
- Information hoarding and credit-claiming creep in where openness used to be.
- The annual conversation feels simultaneously high-stakes and disconnected from the actual work.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The individual performance review is not a neutral administrative ritual; it is structurally destructive in a team that's trying to self-manage. Its whole mechanism — one person above the work evaluating and ranking the individuals below — reinstates the personal dominance hierarchy that DP1 is built on. It tells everyone, unambiguously, where power actually sits and what actually gets rewarded: not the team's outcome, but each individual's standing with their boss.

That signal directly contradicts the conditions a DP2, self-managing team depends on. Self-management requires people to coordinate peer-to-peer, share freely, and own a whole task collectively. Individual evaluation pulls in the opposite direction, and the research is consistent: external individual assessment undermines exactly the intrinsic motivation that DP2 structures generate. So the organisation spends the year building collaborative conditions and then, once a year, runs a process engineered to dismantle them.

This is why "fixing the review" — better forms, calibration sessions, 360 feedback — misses the point. The dysfunction isn't in how the review is conducted; it's that an individual review *exists at all* inside a structure meant to manage performance collectively. The annual competitive turn isn't a culture problem to coach away. It's the rational response to a structural signal about where reward and power live.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — let the group manage its own performance, and remove individual review entirely.**
- In a genuinely self-managing team, the group sets and reviews its own goals collectively; the individual appraisal becomes unnecessary and is dropped.
- Decouple pay and progression from a manager's individual rating so the structure stops rewarding personal positioning over collective outcome.
- Make performance a continuous, peer-owned conversation, not an annual verdict delivered from above.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Strip ranking and forced distribution out of whatever review you're stuck with; minimise its weight on pay and standing.
- Shift as much of the conversation as you can toward team outcomes and peer feedback rather than individual comparison.
- Reframe the seasonal slump: people aren't suddenly selfish — the structure just asked them to be, for a few weeks, and they complied.
