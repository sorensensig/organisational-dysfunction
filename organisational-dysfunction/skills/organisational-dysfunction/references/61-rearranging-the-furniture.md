# Rearranging the furniture

*Dysfunction `#61` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7478322822759682049-PKVk) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The organisation keeps launching job-enrichment initiatives: adding variety, clarity, learning opportunities, and feedback loops to individual roles.
- Things improve for a while — then fade, and a few years later the same programme comes back under a new name.
- Underneath, the work is structurally unchanged: people are still doing work that someone else designed for them.
- It feels like progress, but it's rearranging the furniture in a room whose walls never move.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

This is a **DP1 solution applied to a DP1 problem** — it improves the parts without changing the system. Job enrichment makes an individual role more comfortable and varied, but it leaves the defining feature of DP1 intact: **someone above the work still designs the work.** Management or HR redesigns the roles; the people doing the job remain recipients of a design handed down.

The decisive variable is *who designs the work*. Norway's Industrial Democracy Programme in the 1960s found that work designed by **self-managing groups** consistently outperformed individually enriched roles — because only collective ownership of the work satisfies all six of Emery and Trist's psychological requirements for productive work (autonomy, learning, variety, mutual support and respect, meaningfulness, and a desirable future). Enrich a role from above and you can hit a couple of those; you can't manufacture ownership by decree.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — move the design of the work to the people doing it.**
- Shift from management-designed roles to **self-managing teams that design their own work, their own coordination, and their own goals.** Nobody hands it down.
- Treat "who controls work design" as the actual lever. If that doesn't change, you're improving comfort within fixed constraints, and the effect will decay like every enrichment programme before it.

**If you can't restructure yet:**
- Be honest about what a fresh enrichment initiative can and can't do — name that it will fade unless ownership of the design moves, so people don't mistake motion for change.
- Start handing real design decisions (how the work is done, how it's coordinated) to the team wherever you have the authority to, rather than optimising the role on their behalf.
