# Them and us

*Dysfunction `#46` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7470724103147266049-Ocym) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- A hard line runs between groups — IT vs. business, dev vs. ops, HQ vs. the regions, management vs. staff — and everyone knows which side they're on.
- People speak about the other group with mild contempt or weary resignation: "they don't get it," "they never deliver."
- Cross-group collaboration is effortful, slow, and faintly adversarial even when everyone is acting in good faith.
- Workshops, offsites, and leadership "we're all one team" messaging give a brief warm glow, then the divide reasserts itself.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The in-group/out-group split is not a culture problem — it is the predictable output of a DP1 structure. When you organise separate functions, each with its own manager, its own goals, and its own metrics, you have built the conditions that manufacture "them and us." Kurt Lewin's group-dynamics work named the mechanism decades ago: structure shapes communication, and communication shapes the felt boundary between groups.

Two patterns do the damage. **Asymmetry**: communication runs vertically by status, up and down chains, with almost no genuine peer conversation across the boundary — so the other group is known only through their manager's filtered account. **Egocentrism**: when accountability is individual and goals are local, the rational move is to protect your own group's numbers, not to share information that might help theirs. Put those together and contempt is simply the affective residue of a structure that rewards each side for not understanding the other.

This is why the workshops don't stick. They treat the feeling as the problem and try to improve the relationship directly. But the structure keeps regenerating the asymmetry and egocentrism every working day, so the goodwill drains as fast as a retreat can top it up. You cannot relationship your way out of a structural divide.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — remove the conditions that produce the divide, don't manage the feeling.**
- Reorganise around whole tasks with shared goals, so the people who used to face each other across a boundary now share one outcome.
- Make the relations peer negotiations rather than status-based hand-offs — DP2 coordination dissolves the up-and-over communication path that fed the asymmetry.
- Align metrics to the shared task so no group is rewarded for hoarding information from another.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Create real peer contact across the line: put the people who actually do the work in direct conversation, bypassing the manager-to-manager filter that breeds caricature.
- Find or build a genuinely shared goal at the edges — a joint outcome both sides own — even if the org chart still separates you.
- Reframe the contempt for your team: the other group isn't lazy or obstructive; they're responding rationally to goals and incentives someone else set. The enemy is the design, not the people across it.
