# Professional leadership

*Dysfunction `#26` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7460218662335975424--veI) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The organisation invests heavily in leadership — development programmes, coaching, competency frameworks — and yet teams still underperform.
- Engagement is mediocre, decisions crawl, and good people leave, even though the managers are capable and well-intentioned.
- Each disappointing result triggers more leadership investment: another framework, another offsite, another round of 360s.
- Leadership quality is plainly not the bottleneck — so the question hangs unanswered: if the leaders are good, what's wrong?

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The role itself is the dysfunction. In a **DP1** design, coordination and control live in a management layer *above* the productive work. That structure makes good leadership necessary — someone has to integrate the fragments — but it cannot solve the underlying problem, which is that the people doing the work are not in control of it. No amount of leadership skill removes that constraint, because the constraint is the structure the leadership exists to operate.

So leadership development treats a symptom while leaving the cause untouched. You can train the best managers in the world and still get slow decisions and low engagement, because the bottleneck isn't the quality of the person in the role — it's the *existence* of the role as the place where coordination has to happen. The better the leader, the more effectively they run a structure that holds the work away from the people doing it.

In a **DP2** design, teams handle their own coordination and control, and the professional-leadership role becomes largely redundant. That's the uncomfortable implication: the problem isn't finding better leaders within DP1; it's that DP1 needs them at all.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — build self-managing capacity, not better managers.**
- Redirect the leadership-development budget toward developing teams' ability to coordinate and control their own work (**DP2**).
- Treat it as a paradigm shift, not a tweak — a change in where authority lives, which makes much of the traditional leadership role unnecessary.
- Measure success by how few decisions need to climb the hierarchy, not by how skilfully managers handle the ones that do.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Use whatever leadership latitude you have to *give away* coordination — push decisions to the team rather than centralising them in yourself.
- Reframe your role from "the person who decides" to "the person who removes the need to escalate."
- Be honest in reviews that flat results aren't a leadership-skill gap; that protects both managers and teams from chasing the wrong fix.
