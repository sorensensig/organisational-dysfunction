# Empowerment

*Dysfunction `#36` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7465663011999973376-C8aK) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Leadership announces "empowerment": new principles, managers rebranded as coaches, controls loosened, warmer communication encouraged.
- There's a burst of genuine energy — and then teams try to actually *use* it.
- The approval chains, the budget gates, the sign-offs are all exactly where they were. Nothing real has moved.
- The first time something slips — a missed deadline, a budget overrun — the "coach" reverts to manager and reasserts control.
- People conclude, correctly, that the empowerment was a vocabulary change, and quietly stop spending energy on it.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

This is the Trainer/Leader/Coach pattern: the tone softens, the language warms, the controls relax a little — and the design principle never changes. Underneath the coaching vocabulary the structure is still DP1. Authority over coordination and goals still lives above the team; only the manner of exercising it has been redecorated.

Which is why the crisis is so revealing. When something goes wrong, the organisation falls back on its *only legitimate* source of authority, and that authority is the DP1 bureaucracy. It kicks back into life not because the manager is acting in bad faith but because no other structure exists to hold the moment. The reversion is the structure asserting itself, exactly as designed. The warmth was real; it was just never load-bearing.

Worse, loosening control *without* relocating it doesn't produce empowerment — it produces laissez-faire. Teams get apparent freedom while the real constraints sit untouched, waiting. People are told they're empowered and then discover, the hard way, that the goals and the coordination were never theirs. That gap between the announcement and the structure is the dysfunction.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — empowerment means the team genuinely controls the coordination and goals of its own work; anything less is just a nicer way of saying DP1.**
- Move real authority over goals, priorities and budget into the team — a DP2 shift in who decides, not a change in tone.
- Dismantle the approval and budget gates that contradict the announcement, rather than leaving them parked above the team.
- Test it against the crisis: if the structure that reasserts itself when things go wrong is the hierarchy, the team was never empowered.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Don't mistake loosened control for transferred authority — locate exactly which decisions the team actually owns, and work within that honestly.
- Name the laissez-faire risk: "we've been given freedom but not control of goals/budget" — so people don't burn energy on a freedom that will snap back.
- Push for one real decision right to move down for good; a single genuinely owned decision beats a charter full of warm words.
