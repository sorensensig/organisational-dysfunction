# Quiet quitting

*Dysfunction `#8` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7448345016785211392-jC05) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- People do exactly what the job description says and not one thing more.
- No volunteering, no initiative, no staying late for the stretch — the extra discretionary effort has quietly gone.
- Engagement surveys show a small engaged minority and a meaningful actively-disengaged group, with most people coasting in between.
- Pay, hours, and job security are fine, yet the energy and ownership simply aren't there.
- Managers reach for perks, pep talks, or motivation campaigns, and nothing moves.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The reflex is to treat quiet quitting as an attitude problem to be fixed in the person. Sociotechnically it's the opposite: it's a design problem showing up as behaviour. Discretionary effort is fuelled by *intrinsic* motivators, and these are properties of how the work is arranged — not of pay or policy.

Hjorteland anchors this in the six psychological requirements of productive work: room to make decisions and own a meaningful chunk of it (elbow room and ownership); ongoing learning; variety; mutual support and respect from colleagues; meaningfulness — seeing how the work connects to something worth doing; and a desirable future, a path forward. When the work is designed so these are present, engagement follows almost automatically. When the design starves them, engagement collapses — and crucially, no amount of extrinsic compensation refills it. You can have good pay, sane hours, and total security, and people will still disengage, because the things that produce engagement were never on offer.

So quiet quitting is a rational, predictable response to job architecture, not a character flaw or a generational failing. Chasing it with motivation initiatives is treating the symptom; the variable that actually moves is the design of the work itself.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — redesign the work so the six intrinsic requirements are actually met.**
- Give teams real ownership of a whole, meaningful piece of work, with the authority to make the decisions inside it (DP2).
- Build in learning and variety — rotation, growth, new challenges — rather than fixed, narrow, repetitive roles.
- Design for support and respect between colleagues, a visible line from the work to something that matters, and a credible path forward.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Within your own team, widen people's decision latitude, vary assignments, and make the purpose of the work explicit — several of the six are partly in a team's gift.
- Cultivate genuine peer support and respect locally; that requirement doesn't need a reorg to start improving.
- Reframe with leadership: disengagement here is a signal about job design, not employee virtue. More perks won't fix a structural deficit — and saying so redirects energy to where it can actually work.
