# Designed to undermine

*Dysfunction `#53` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7474708703519289344-U-bt) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- You stand up self-managing teams, performance lifts for a while, then slowly slides back toward old hierarchical habits — with no formal redesign or rollback.
- People quietly return to looking after their own slice, even though the team chart says "shared ownership".
- The team works fine on its own terms but keeps colliding with HR, finance, and management processes that assume something else.
- Nobody can point to the decision that reversed the change; it just erodes.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The teams were redesigned to DP2 — control and coordination sitting with the people doing the work — but the surrounding support systems were never touched. Performance reviews, career ladders, bonuses, hiring criteria, and training budgets all still encode DP1 assumptions about individual accountability. Those systems are not neutral. They emit a constant signal about what the organisation actually rewards, and that signal pulls steadily against the new structure until the structure gives way.

This is why a partial transformation is not a safe halfway house. A DP2 island surrounded by DP1 systems is a plant in the wrong soil — the roots have nowhere to go. You cannot run DP2 in one part of the organisation while DP1 governs everything that grades, pays, and promotes the people in it; what you get is not a gentle blend but laissez-faire, where the new structure has responsibility without the supporting design.

Crucially, the failure looks like a people problem ("the team lost discipline", "they reverted") when it is a systems problem. The reversion is the rational response to incentives that never changed. Half a transformation here is often worse than none, because it discredits DP2 while never actually testing it.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — the support systems have to be redesigned to reinforce collective work, not merely tolerate it.**
- Audit every system that touches the team — appraisal, reward, promotion, hiring, budgets — and surface where each still assumes the individual as the unit of accountability.
- Move the unit of measurement and reward to the team and the whole piece of work it owns.
- Sequence the change so structure and support systems move together; don't ship the team redesign and leave the plumbing on DP1.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Name the misalignment explicitly so the inevitable backslide isn't blamed on the team's character or commitment.
- Negotiate local exceptions where you have standing — team-level goals, shared retro actions, collective recognition — to blunt the individualising signals.
- Be honest upward that the experiment is being undermined by design, and that protecting it requires changing systems above the team's pay grade.
