# Communication problems

*Dysfunction `#12` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7451328245641371648-M0n_) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- "We have a communication problem" becomes the catch-all explanation for missed handoffs, delays, and rework.
- Information arrives late, partial, or via the wrong channel; people are "out of the loop" through no clear fault.
- The standard remedy is communication training, comms plans, and more meetings to "improve alignment."
- It never quite gets better — the same breakdowns recur with different people, which somehow doesn't prompt anyone to question the diagnosis.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

OST is blunt here: these communication problems are endemic to DP1 structures and have essentially nothing to do with anyone's ability to communicate. Blaming the skill misreads a structural fact as a personal one.

In a hierarchy, work that needs to flow between people routes *upward* through non-reciprocal intermediaries — you don't talk peer-to-peer with the person you depend on, you escalate to a policymaker who relays it back down. Every one of those asymmetrical hops is a place for delay, distortion, and loss. The number of connection points that *must* be maintained explodes, and each is a relationship of unequal power rather than mutual obligation. A DP2 structure dissolves most of that: it replaces the up-and-over routing with reciprocal, peer-to-peer relationships between teams that own whole pieces of work.

The Metcalfe's-law fear — more people means unmanageably more connections — only bites when people *don't* share goals and must coordinate through control. When peers share a purpose, communication between them is efficient by default; the negotiation is between equals, not across the asymmetry of rank. So sending people on a communication course optimises the wrong variable. You're polishing the skill while leaving in place the structure that guarantees the failure.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — cut the connection points and make the remaining ones reciprocal.**
- Restructure so teams own whole pieces of work and coordinate peer-to-peer, instead of routing everything up through managers.
- Give interdependent teams shared goals, so coordination is driven by common purpose rather than enforced through control.
- Remove the non-reciprocal intermediaries that turn every handoff into an escalation.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Map the actual handoff path for a recurring failure — make the up-and-over routing visible so it's clearly the cause, not the people.
- Build direct peer relationships across team boundaries where you can, bypassing the escalation chain for the work you control.
- When "communication" gets blamed, push back: the people are communicating fine; the wiring forces the breakdown.
