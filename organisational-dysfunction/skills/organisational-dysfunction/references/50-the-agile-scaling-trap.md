# The agile scaling trap

*Dysfunction `#50` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7472890874348158976-odjT) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Agile works on a few teams, so the organisation "scales" it — adopting SAFe or similar across hundreds of developers and dozens of teams.
- In come Release Trains, quarterly PI planning, and a fresh layer of roles: Release Train Engineers, Solution Architects, Portfolio Managers.
- The teams are still doing agile at the bottom. The organisation around them now looks remarkably like the bureaucracy agile was meant to replace.
- The Release Train is a project in disguise; PI planning is quarterly waterfall; the portfolio layer is the old PMO with new names.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

Scaling frameworks are a reassertion of DP1 over the partial DP2 that agile attempted. Agile genuinely pushed some control down to teams — but it never solved coordination *at scale*. It left a real gap: how do many self-managing teams align without a boss above them? Into that unsolved gap, the only answer the organisation knows how to reach for is hierarchy. So the coordination problem gets "solved" by reasserting control one level above the work — which is precisely DP1.

This is DP1 fighting back, and it fights back because nothing structural was actually changed. The people who control resources and budgets still control them; the scaling framework gives that control a modern vocabulary and a cadence, but the design principle underneath is untouched. Quarterly planning, train engineers, and a portfolio layer are DP1 reconstituting itself around teams that were only ever partially DP2. Half a transformation curdles into the thing it set out to escape.

The honest diagnosis is that agile needed *fixing* — specifically, it needed a real answer to coordination across many teams. Instead it got *scaled*. Bolting a bureaucratic framework on top doesn't supply the missing structure; it supplies the missing structure's opposite.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — solve cross-team coordination with a hierarchy of functions, not a hierarchy of control.**
- Build self-managing groups at every level, each owning its whole task and coordinating with the others as peers — DP2 that goes all the way up, not just at the bottom.
- Replace the portfolio/PI/train apparatus with peer-negotiated coordination between teams, so alignment doesn't require a controlling layer above.
- Address resource and budget control directly; if those stay centralised, DP1 will keep reasserting itself no matter what the teams practise.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Name what the framework actually is: a reassertion of control, not a scaling of autonomy. Don't let the vocabulary disguise it.
- Protect the genuine DP2 at team level from the ceremony overhead the framework imposes, and resist the new roles quietly recreating command chains.
- Reframe for teams losing faith: agile didn't fail and you didn't fail it — the unsolved coordination problem got answered with bureaucracy instead of design.
