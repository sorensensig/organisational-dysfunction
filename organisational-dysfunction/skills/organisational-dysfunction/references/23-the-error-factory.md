# The error factory

*Dysfunction `#23` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7458402858888069120-lNPZ) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The same kinds of errors keep recurring despite post-mortems, fixes, and ever-tighter oversight.
- The response is always *more*: more checklists, more process, more training, more sign-off — and the error rate barely moves.
- Leadership concludes people aren't following procedure, so it adds supervision; the mistakes continue anyway.
- Information that would catch problems early gets quietly massaged on its way up, so the people who could act find out too late.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The diagnosis "people need more training" is almost always wrong, and the prescription that follows it won't reliably lower the error rate. The error rate is a property of the **structure**, not the competence of the people.

Two structural effects in **DP1** are at work. First, *compounding*: when sound judgement has to pass through a chain before it becomes a decision, individual reliability multiplies into collective unreliability. If five people are each right eight times out of ten, the odds they're *unanimously* right collapse to roughly one in three — and a hierarchy that demands aligned sign-off is buying that bad math. Second, *asymmetry and competition*: a DP1 structure gives people incentives to filter information to protect their position rather than to surface the truth, so mistakes get hidden exactly where visibility matters most. The structure both manufactures errors and conceals them.

A **DP2** structure inverts both. When a team shares responsibility for the whole outcome, an error becomes a learning opportunity rather than a liability to bury — it's in nobody's interest to hide it, and the information flows freely enough to be caught early. The difference in error rate isn't a difference in people; it's a difference in what the design rewards.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — stop adding oversight to a structure that breeds and hides errors, and change the structure.**
- Give whole-task ownership to **self-managing teams (DP2)** so responsibility for the outcome is shared and there's no incentive to conceal a mistake.
- Collapse sign-off chains: every additional approval node adds compounding unreliability, not safety.
- Make error a source of learning, not blame — surface it close to the work, where the people who caused it can also fix the conditions.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Run blameless post-mortems that ask "what about the conditions made this error likely?" not "who failed to follow procedure?"
- Notice when you're about to add a checklist to fix a structural problem — more process on a DP1 base usually adds friction without reducing error.
- Shorten the path between detecting a problem and acting on it for the part of the system you control.
