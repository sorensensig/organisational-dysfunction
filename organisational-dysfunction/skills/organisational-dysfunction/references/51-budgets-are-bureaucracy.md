# Budgets are bureaucracy

*Dysfunction `#51` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7473249437839085569-icFA) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- You've restructured into autonomous product teams that own their roadmap and how they work together — and then handed them a centralised annual budget.
- Teams forecast their needs 12–18 months out and write business cases to justify their own existence.
- They compete against each other for a fixed pot; mid-year, money gets cut and reallocated through a process they have no part in.
- The autonomy on paper meets the finance department in practice, and everyone knows which one wins.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

Whoever controls the allocation of money controls the structure, regardless of what the org chart says. You can draw the most beautiful DP2 diagram of self-managing teams owning whole tasks, but if the money those teams need is decided a year in advance by a central process they don't participate in, the teams aren't owning the whole task — they're operating inside spaces money already carved out for them. Owning your work means owning the resources for your work; without that, autonomy is decorative.

So a DP2 design with DP1 budgeting is not a DP2 organisation. It is a DP1 organisation with self-managing teams bolted on. The annual budget round is itself a pure DP1 mechanism: control of the most consequential decision — what gets resourced — held well above the work, exercised through forecasting, justification, and competition for scarcity. The structure of money is the real structure of the organisation, and here it stays bureaucratic while the org chart pretends otherwise.

This is the classic half-transformation: the visible, cheap part (team shapes, ceremonies, roadmaps) gets changed; the invisible, powerful part (who allocates the money) does not. And because the money structure is the deeper one, it quietly overrides the redesign every budget cycle.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — design the structure of money and the structure of the organisation together, by the people who'll live with both.**
- Move resource control toward the teams that own the work, so they genuinely own the whole task, money included.
- Don't have leadership impose a new funding model from the top — that's just DP1 again. Use OST methods like the Search Conference and Participative Design Workshop so the people affected design how money flows.
- Treat Beyond Budgeting as a step in the right direction — away from the fixed annual forecast — but recognise the goal is a co-designed money structure, not just a new finance technique.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Name the contradiction plainly: a DP2 org chart with DP1 budgeting is a DP1 org. Stop calling the teams autonomous when finance holds the real lever.
- Win whatever local control you can over how allocated money is spent, and shorten forecasting horizons where finance will allow it.
- Reframe for teams demoralised by mid-year cuts: the powerlessness isn't a failure of your planning; it's built into who holds the money, and that needs changing above your level.
