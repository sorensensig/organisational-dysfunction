# The product owner trap

*Dysfunction `#38` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7467092605852340224-8L76) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The team has a product owner sitting right there with them, yet she mostly relays decisions made by a stakeholder who sits outside the team.
- Raising a concern means a three-week round trip: up to the real decision-maker and back, through the proxy.
- The team builds what it's told to build. It has opinions about *what* would be valuable but no authority to act on them.
- Everyone can see it's a feature factory. Nobody knows what to do about it, because the constraint isn't visible in any one person's behaviour.
- The "product owner" title signals empowerment that the role's actual authority doesn't contain.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

This is pure bureaucracy — DP1 — wearing a product label. The defining feature of DP1 is that responsibility for goal-setting sits *above* the work; it doesn't matter whether that's one step above or twenty. A proxy product owner formalises exactly that: she is the one step of separation between the team and the authority to decide what's worth doing. The old ordering-supplier divide between "the business" and "IT" hasn't been dissolved, just renamed and seated closer.

A genuinely self-managing team (DP2) owns its *whole* task — not only how the work gets done but what work gets done and why. Marty Cagan's distinction is the same line drawn from the product side: a feature team is handed a solution to build; an empowered product team is handed a problem to solve. The gap between those two is structural, not a matter of better backlog refinement or a more senior PO. You can give the role a great title and a seat in the team room and still have changed nothing, because the authority to set goals never moved.

So the symptom — disengagement, slow loops, "we just build what we're told" — isn't a motivation failure or a skills gap. It's the rational, predictable behaviour of people whose structure denies them the very ownership the framework promises them.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — move goal-setting authority *inside* the team, or stop calling it empowered.**
- Give the team a problem and an outcome to own, not a pre-decided solution to deliver; let it decide what to build.
- Establish a direct relationship between the team and the actual customer/stakeholder, so understanding isn't laundered through a proxy.
- Make the product owner a full member who sets direction *with* the team, not a courier carrying decisions from elsewhere.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Make the loop visible: track and surface the cost of the three-week round trips so the structural constraint becomes undeniable to those who can change it.
- Get the team direct, even informal, contact with users to shorten the gap between work and understanding.
- Reframe the blame: the PO isn't failing and the team isn't lazy — the design put a translation layer where ownership should be.
