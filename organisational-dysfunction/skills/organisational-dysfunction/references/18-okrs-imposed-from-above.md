# OKRs imposed from above

*Dysfunction `#18` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7453844533240430592-35xw) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Leadership sets company objectives that cascade down through departments to teams.
- The framework says teams "own their goals," but in practice team-level OKRs are determined by the level above; the team mostly translates management's objectives into local language.
- Quarterly check-ins become a compliance ritual — scoring and reporting — rather than genuine commitment.
- The scoring quietly turns into a performance-review proxy, so people game targets or set safe ones.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

OKRs are "a good idea applied in the wrong structure." Goal-setting is one of the **core functions of a self-managing (DP2) team** — something the team *does*, grounded in its own understanding of its work and environment. Cascading objectives top-down is **DP1 control wearing a DP2 costume**: the authority to decide what matters still lives above the work, and the team is left executing, not owning.

Because the objectives aren't really the team's, the commitment isn't real either — you get translation and compliance instead of ownership. And once scores feed appraisal, you've layered an individual-control mechanism onto collective work (see *The performance review*), which corrodes the very autonomy the framework promised.

## What to do

**The real fix: let goals emerge from the team, inside a shared purpose.**
- Team goals should arise from the team's own reading of its work, its customers/environment, and a shared organisational purpose **the team helped shape** — not from a number handed down. That different starting point is what produces genuine commitment rather than compliance.
- Leadership's job is to make the organisation's direction and constraints clear and real (see *The company's strategy is unclear*), then trust teams to set objectives that serve it — not to author the teams' objectives for them.
- Decouple goal-setting from individual appraisal and scoring. The moment an OKR is a stick, it stops being a sense-making tool.

**If you can't change the cascade yet:**
- Use the imposed objectives as *context*, and let the team author its own objectives and key results underneath them — shifting as much real choice as possible to the team.
- Drop the theatre: stop scoring OKRs for performance management; use them only to focus and learn.
