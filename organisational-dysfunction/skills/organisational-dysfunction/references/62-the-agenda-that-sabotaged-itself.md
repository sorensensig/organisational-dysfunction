# The agenda that sabotaged itself

*Dysfunction `#62` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-ugcPost-7478745688902774784-BsWm) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- A workshop opens with real energy — a good keynote, then productive small-group work — and everyone's engaged.
- Midway it curdles. A senior-leader panel gives feedback, questions turn pointed, a few people go quiet.
- It ends with winners and losers instead of consensus, and people leave drained — even though the facilitation was skilled and everyone came in good faith.
- Afterwards it gets blamed on personalities, "difficult" participants, or the facilitator having "lost the room."

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The problem isn't the people or the facilitation — it's built into the **agenda**, which silently switches design principles partway through. The keynote runs on **DP1** (an expert transmits, the room depends and receives). The group work runs on **DP2** (participants own the work and coordinate as peers). Then the leader panel snaps back to **DP1** (a hierarchy evaluates what the peers produced). Each switch changes *who is responsible for what* without anyone naming it.

That inconsistency is the sabotage. As Merrelyn Emery put it, **"mixed mode is not a synthesis or a balance between the design principles"** — it's just incoherence. Every shift resets the group's sense of who's in charge, and Bion's basic assumptions get triggered fresh; the panel in particular reactivates the hierarchy and pulls the room into fight-or-flight. Skilled facilitation can't paper over a structure that keeps contradicting itself. As Hjorteland puts it: **the problem is not in the room — it's on the agenda.**

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — design the whole event on a single design principle and keep it there.**
- Pick one and hold it end to end. For a participative session that means DP2 throughout (e.g. the Search Conference / Participative Design tradition): people own the analysis *and* the conclusions, with no expert panel bolted on to judge them.
- If leaders must be involved, put them *in* the DP2 work as participants, not above it as an evaluating panel — the moment a hierarchy sits in judgement, you're back in DP1 and the mode has flipped.
- Design the agenda backwards from "who is responsible for the output" and make sure that answer never changes mid-event.

**If you can't redesign the whole agenda yet:**
- Spot the switch points in advance (the keynote→groups→panel handoffs) — those are where the room will turn — and remove or reframe the DP1 pieces (drop the evaluating panel; make the keynote a shared input, not a verdict).
- Recovering after the room has already turned is hard, so prevention beats rescue; if it's turning, name what happened ("we just shifted who owns this") rather than facilitating harder.
