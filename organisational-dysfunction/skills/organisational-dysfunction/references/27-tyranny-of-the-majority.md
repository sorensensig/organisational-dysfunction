# Tyranny of the majority

*Dysfunction `#27` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-activity-7460603573530243072-soOu) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- A workshop closes with dot-voting or a show of hands. There's a clear winner, the room moves on, and it feels democratic.
- The minority who lost don't just lose the vote — they lose any further say in the outcome. They walk out with a decision they had no real part in shaping.
- Commitment to implementation is thin: the "losers" comply at best, and the decision needs chasing and enforcement to actually happen.
- Over time people learn the vote is where their concerns go to die, and they stop bringing the real objections into the room at all.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

Majority and dot-voting feel participatory, but they're **representative-democracy mechanisms — DP1 in disguise**. They resolve disagreement by *counting* rather than by *understanding*, which manufactures winners and losers. And the loser doesn't merely lose this decision; they lose influence over it entirely, which is precisely the condition that kills ownership.

This matters because commitment comes from having genuinely shaped the outcome, not from having been outvoted on it. A decision that 60% wanted and 40% had no hand in is a decision 40% have no reason to own — so it gets implemented half-heartedly and needs external enforcement, which is exactly the overhead self-management is supposed to remove. The vote produces a *result* but not *shared commitment*, and those are not the same thing.

The alternative is Fred Emery's **rationalisation of conflict**: stay with the disagreement long enough to actually understand it, rather than forcing it to a quick numerical resolution. The aim shifts from "the most popular option" to "the option nobody has a reasoned objection to." That's slower up front, but it produces genuine shared ownership — and a group that owns its decision implements it without anyone having to enforce it.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — resolve disagreement by understanding it, not by counting it.**
- Replace majority voting with **consent-style** decisions: seek the option nobody has a *reasoned objection* to, rather than the one most hands go up for.
- Treat dissent as information about the decision, not an obstacle to it — surface and work through objections rather than outvoting them.
- Locate decision authority in the **self-managing team** that has to live with and implement the outcome, so ownership and execution sit together.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- When you must use a vote, don't stop there — give the minority a real channel to shape the *how*, so they keep some influence over the outcome.
- Spend the time to understand objections before resolving; "we voted" is not the same as "we agreed."
- Watch for the silent objection: if people stop raising concerns, it's because counting taught them not to bother.
