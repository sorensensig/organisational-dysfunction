# The daily status report

*Dysfunction `#1` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_sociotechnical-agile-sts-share-7447223490782388224-nyhV) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- The daily standup feels like "a drag and a complete waste of time."
- People recite what they did yesterday and will do today, but nobody really listens — because another person's work has little bearing on their own.
- The standup is effectively a **status report to a manager** (or to the ritual itself), not a coordination conversation between peers.
- Attendance is dutiful; energy is low; the meeting is defended as "the process."

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The standup is a DP2 ritual (a self-managing team synchronising its shared work) bolted onto a **DP1 reality**: a group of individuals each assigned a separate slice of work by someone above them. If members are working on genuinely independent tasks, there is nothing to coordinate — so the meeting *correctly* feels useless. The boredom is information, not a facilitation failure.

In other words, this isn't a team that runs a bad standup; it's **a group that isn't a team**, running a ceremony that only makes sense for a team. Work was divided to maximise individual efficiency (DP1, redundancy of parts), so the daily sync has no shared object to organise around.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — give the group shared work to coordinate.**
- Organise the team around a whole, meaningful piece of work (a product, a customer outcome, a value stream) that genuinely requires them to depend on each other, rather than parallel individual assignments.
- Let coordination be peer-to-peer about *that* shared work. When there is real interdependence, a daily sync becomes useful on its own merits and you won't have to mandate it.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- Stop running the standup as a status round to a lead. Drop "yesterday/today/blockers" recitation; talk only about work that actually crosses people.
- Treat low engagement as a diagnostic, not a discipline problem — surface it: "this meeting isn't useful because we're not really working on the same thing — why is that?" That conversation is more valuable than the standup.
- Resist the instinct to make the ritual stricter or better-facilitated; that treats the symptom and entrenches the DP1 split.
