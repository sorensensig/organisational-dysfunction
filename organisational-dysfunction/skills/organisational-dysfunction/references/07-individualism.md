# Individualism

*Dysfunction `#7` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7447941074641256448-aizO) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- Wins are pinned on individual heroes; failures are pinned on individual scapegoats.
- A "hero culture" rewards the person who pulls the all-nighter and ignores the system that made the all-nighter necessary.
- Stretch goals are handed to individuals, isolating them with risk they can't fully control.
- Leaders who are literally paid to be accountable somehow escape consequences when things go wrong.
- Post-mortems end at "who messed up" and never reach "what about the situation made this likely."

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

Open Systems Theory takes the group, not the individual, as the basic unit of working life. Treating people as standalone, context-free performers contradicts that — and it isn't just unfair, it's analytically blinding. When you frame outcomes as the product of individuals, you erase the social and technical system they were operating inside. And once the system is invisible, the individual is the *only* thing left to blame.

Hjorteland's sharp point is that this doesn't merely blame victims — it *manufactures* them. By design, individualism leaves nothing else to hold responsible, so a person must absorb what was really a systemic failure. The hero and the scapegoat are the same mechanism running in opposite directions: both attribute to one person what the whole sociotechnical context produced. Chasing individual accountability this way is a Goodhart trap — you optimise the appearance of clear responsibility while degrading your ability to actually learn.

It also misreads what people need. They need *both* autonomy and belonging — a paradox the group resolves: inside a real team, the individual is protected, can take risks, and can fail safely because the unit owns the result together.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — move praise, blame, and accountability to the group that actually owns the work.**
- Make the team the unit of accountability: it owns its goals and outcomes collectively, so success and failure are shared and learnable.
- Design work so individuals are protected inside the group rather than exposed alone to risks they don't control.
- Hold leaders to system-level accountability — for the conditions they set — not just downstream individuals.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- In reviews and retros, push every "who" toward a "what in the situation" — make the system visible even when the org wants a name.
- Credit outcomes to the team in public; refuse to let a single scapegoat carry a collective failure.
- Reframe for the people around you: isolating individuals doesn't reveal the truth, it hides the system. Protect each other and the learning improves.
