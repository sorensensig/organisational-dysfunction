# Passivity in team workshops

*Dysfunction `#4` in Trond Hjorteland's ["Organisational Dysfunction of the Day"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/trondhjort_opensystemstheory-sociotechnical-orgdesign-share-7448280337383772160-tLrp) series — synthesised through open sociotechnical systems theory; paraphrased, not quoted.*

## How it shows up

- People who are sharp and opinionated in the corridor go quiet the moment a workshop starts.
- The room defers to the most senior person present, even when others know the material better.
- Ideas get floated tentatively, then dropped the instant a manager frowns or stays silent.
- Genuine disagreement is swallowed; the group converges fast on whatever the powerful person seems to favour.
- Participants leave feeling the session was "fine" but suspiciously thin on real contribution.

## The sociotechnical diagnosis

The trouble isn't that the people are passive; it's that authority is in the room. When a formal leader with power over careers is present, the group stops doing the work and starts managing the threat. Hjorteland reaches for Bion's *basic assumptions* here: under the pressure of hierarchy the group slips into dependency (wait for the leader to decide), fight/flight, or pairing — unconscious group-level coping that crowds out the actual task.

This is a DP1 dynamic intruding on a DP2 activity. A workshop only works if knowledge and arguments win on merit; but in the presence of positional power, people submit to the *position*, not the expertise or the better case. That submission is rational — disagreeing with someone who controls your prospects is genuinely costly. So the silence is a sane response to the power dynamics, not a deficit of engagement or skill.

The standard "fixes" — better icebreakers, urging people to speak up, more psychological-safety talk — leave the cause untouched. The cause is who is standing at the front of the room.

## What to do

**The real fix is structural — design the workshop as DP2 regardless of how the org is shaped the rest of the time.**
- Keep appointed leaders out of working sessions where the team needs to think freely; their absence is what lets expertise outrank rank.
- Let the group own the task itself — its framing, its options, its conclusions — so authority sits with the work, not a person.
- Where a decision genuinely needs the leader, have the team do the thinking first and bring a recommendation, rather than thinking *in front of* the boss.

**If you can't change the structure yet:**
- If you're the senior person, leave the room — or at minimum speak last, never first, and visibly defer to the work.
- Use methods that decouple ideas from status: silent writing, anonymous input, round-robins, dot-voting, so contributions aren't filtered through the hierarchy in real time.
- Name the dynamic gently with the team: the quiet isn't apathy, it's a reasonable read of who holds power. That makes it discussable instead of a personal failing.
